Early and accurate diagnosis of Parkinson ' s disease (PD) remains challenging due to reliance on clinical assessment of motor symptoms. This retrospective study compared the diagnostic performance of neuromelanin (NM)-sensitive MRI and free water (FW) imaging in 247 patients with early PD and 78 controls from a single tertiary center, with independent external validation in a separate cohort acquired at another institution. NM volumes were measured in the substantia nigra pars compacta and its functional subregions (limbic, associative, and sensorimotor), while FW values were extracted from corresponding substantia nigra regions using diffusion tensor imaging. A combined NMmodel incorporating subregional volumes was developed through multivariable logistic regression. NM volumes were reduced across all regions in early PD patients compared to controls, while FW values showed no signi fi cant group differences. NM measurements consistently outperformed FW imaging across all regions, with the combined NM model achieving superior diagnostic accuracy, and NM-based measures also demonstrated higher diagnostic performance than FW in the external validation cohort. These results highlight the clinical utility of NM-MRI for early diagnostic assessment and its potential integration into emerging multimodal biomarker frameworks for PD.

Parkinson ' s disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra (SN), manifesting with motor symptoms such as bradykinesia, rigidity, and rest tremor, as well as non-motor features including olfactory de fi cit, rapid eye movement sleep behavior disorder, and autonomic dysfunction 1 -4 . Early and accurate diagnosis is crucial for timely intervention, optimization of treatment strategies, and facilitation of patient recruitment for diseasemodifying clinical trials. However, PD diagnosis currently relies predominantly on the clinical assessment of motor symptoms, with the diagnostic accuracy varying considerably depending on the clinical setting, disease duration, and clinician expertise 5 . This reliance on clinical criteria often leads to delayed diagnosis until a substantial portion of dopaminergic neurons has already been lost 6 . These limitations underscore the need for accurate and objective diagnostic methods for early-stage PD to improve clinical management and enable the monitoring of disease progression.

Advances in MRI have enabled the noninvasive detection of diseaserelated changes in the SN. Free water (FW) imaging is a diffusion MRI technique based on a bitensor model that quanti fi es the fractional volume of extracellular water within a voxel 7 . Increased FW within the SN has been interpreted as an indicator of neurodegeneration, likely re fl ecting neuroin fl ammation, edema, blood-brain barrier disruption, or atrophy 8,9 . Although NM-MRI and FW imaging have each been proposed as biomarkers, direct comparative studies remain scarce, and existing work has often included heterogeneous disease stages or relied on manually de fi ned regions of interest. Consequently, the relative diagnostic utility of these modalities in early PD has not been systematically evaluated. To address this gap, the present study provides a harmonized head-to-head comparison of NM-MRI and FW imaging within a rigorously characterized early PD cohort, using a fully automated subregional analysis pipeline under uniform acquisition and processing conditions. We further strengthen the evaluation by incorporating an external validation cohort. This approach enables a morepreciseassessmentofthecomparativediagnosticperformanceofthese imaging biomarkers in the earliest clinically recognizable phase of PD.

## Diagnostic Performance of FW and NM

The diagnostic performances of FW and NM biomarkers in distinguishing patients with early PD from controls are summarized in Table 2 and Fig. 2. Overall, the NM measurements demonstrated superior diagnostic performance compared to FW across all regions. While FW showed a discriminative ability, with AUC values ranging from 0.605 to 0.62, NM

consistently achieved higher AUC values, ranging from 0.729 to 0.791. Among the NM subregions, the sensorimotor region showed the highest individual performance (AUC = 0.791, 95% CI, 0.733 -0.848), followed by the associative (AUC=0.788; 95% CI, 0.731 -0.844) and limbic regions (AUC=0.729, 95% CI, 0.663 -0.794).

The combined NM model, which incorporated NM volumes from the three subregions using multivariable logistic regression, achieved the highest diagnostic performance, with an AUC of 0.833 (95% CI, 0.784 -0.881). The optimal cut-off point was determined based on the Youden index, yielding a sensitivity of 68.4%, a speci fi city of 87.2%, and an accuracy of 72.9%, with a high positive predictive value (94.4%) and a negative predictive value of 46.6%. The combined NM model outperformed the total or single regional SNpcvolumemeasurementsaccording to DeLong ' s test: total SNpc volume ( p =0.002), associative region ( p =0.006), limbic region ( p &lt; 0.001), and sensorimotor region ( p =0.038).

## SubgroupAnalysisinPatientswithDiseaseDuration ≤ 24Months

To assess diagnostic performance in the earliest disease stages, we strati fi ed patients by disease duration. Among the 247 patients with early PD, 139 (56.3%) had disease duration ≤ 24 months and 108 (43.7%) had disease duration of 25 -60months(SupplementaryTable2).ThetwoPDsubgroups did not differ signi fi cantly in Hoehn and Yahr scale, MDS-UPDRS III scores, or MRI to UPDRS assessment interval (all p &gt; 0.05).

In patients with disease duration ≤ 24 months, NM volumes remained reduced compared to controls across all SNpc regions after FDR correction (all adjusted p &lt; 0.001), while FW measurements showed no signi fi cant group differences (all adjusted p &gt; 0.05) (Supplementary Table 3).

The diagnostic performance in this earliest-stage subgroup demonstrated that, similar to the full early PD cohort, NM measurements consistently outperformed FW across all examined regions (NM AUC: 0.693 -0.759 vs. FW AUC: 0.609 -0.631, Supplementary Table 4 and Supplementary Fig. 1). The combined NM model achieved the highest diagnostic performance with an AUC of 0.796 (95% CI: 0.736 -0.857), outperforming total SNpc and individual regional measurements (all p &lt; 0.05 by DeLong ' s test).

## Correlation between Imaging Measures and Clinical Scales

The correlations between the imaging measures and clinical scales are summarized in Supplementary Table 5. Although FW values showed weak positive correlations with MDS-UPDRS III (total SN: ρ =0.167) and NM volumes showed weak negative correlations with disease duration (total SNpc: ρ = -0.146; limbic: ρ = -0.152), none of these associations were signi fi cant after FDR correction (all FDRp &gt; 0.05).

To clarify the independent contributions of age, sex, and disease duration, two separate multivariable regression analyses were performed for patients with early PD and all participants. In patients with early PD (Supplementary Table 6), older age was associated with higher FW in the total SN ( β =0.0054, p &lt; 0.001) and the associative subregion ( β =0.0014, p =0.003). Male sex was also associated with higher FW values in the total SN ( β =0.058, p =0.006), associative ( β =0.0338, p &lt; 0.001), and limbic ( β =0.0358, p &lt; 0.001) regions. Disease duration demonstrated a positive association only with FW in the sensorimotor region ( β =0.0005, p =0.033). For NM measures, disease duration remained the primary determinant of volume loss across all subregions: total SNpc ( β = -0.0036, p =0.001), associative ( β = -0.0013, p =0.012), limbic ( β = -0.0019, p =0.009), and sensorimotor ( β = -0.0003, p =0.049). Male sex was associated with lower NM volume in the associative region ( β = -0.0385, p =0.02), whereas age did not signi fi cantly in fl uence NM volume in any subregion.

When examining all participants together (Supplementary Table 7), both age and sex showed consistent effects on FW across all subregions. FW increased with age in the total SN ( β =0.0058, p &lt; 0.001), associative ( β =0.0018, p &lt; 0.001), limbic ( β =0.0011, p =0.004), and sensorimotor ( β =0.0008, p =0.03) regions. Male sex was also associated with higher FW values across all regions. NM measures did not show signi fi cant age-related associations, but they demonstrated sex-related differences, with males exhibiting lower NM volumes in the total ( β = -0.0982, p =0.023), associative ( β = -0.0518, p =0.001), and limbic ( β = -0.0463, p =0.039) regions.

## Effect of Antiparkinsonian Medication on NM Volumes

Asasubgroupanalysis,weexaminedthepotentialeffectofantiparkinsonian medication on imaging measures in PD patients with available levodopa equivalent daily dose (LEDD) data (92/247, 37.2%). Of these, 24 (26.1%) were medication-naive (LEDD = 0 mg/day) and 68 (73.9%) were medicated (median LEDD: 300 [150, 450] mg/day).

Comparison between medication-naive and medicated patients showed no signi fi cant differences in NM volume measurements after FDR correction (all p &gt; 0.05) (Supplementary Table 8). In medicated patients, multivariable linear regression analyses adjusting for age, sex, and disease duration con fi rmed these fi ndings, showing no signi fi cant associations between LEDD and NM volumes (all p &gt; 0.05) (Supplementary Table 9).

## Independent External Validation of Diagnostic Performance

Theexternal validation cohort consisted of 69 patients with early PD and 70 disease-control participants, all of whom were diagnosed with essential tremor (Supplementary Table 10). There were no signi fi cant differences in age or sex between the two groups. Among patients with early PD, the median disease duration was 12 months.

In comparisons of FW and NM measures between patients with early PD and controls (Supplementary Table 11 and Supplementary Fig. 2), FW values were higher in the early PD group only in the associative subregion (median, 0.18 vs. 0.15; p =0.008). FW values in the total SN and other subregions were higher in early PD but did not reach statistical signi fi cance. In contrast, NM volumes were signi fi cantly reduced in patients with early PD compared with controls in the total SNpc as well as in all three subregions.

Diagnostic performance in the external validation cohort is summarized in Supplementary Table 12. NM measures demonstrated higher discriminative performance than FW measures, with AUC values ranging from 0.640 to 0.664 for NM and from 0.585 to 0.628 for FW. The combined NM model showed a modest increase in AUC (0.665) compared with individual NM subregional measures; however, this difference was not statistically signi fi cant (DeLong test, all p &gt; 0.05).

## Discussion

In this study, we re fi ne and validate the diagnostic performance of NM and FW -based biomarkers within a strictly de fi ned early PD population. NM volume, both in the total SNpc and across the individual subregions, was reduced in patients with early PD compared to controls, while FW content showed no signi fi cant difference between the groups. NM measurements consistently outperformed FW across regions (AUC: 0.729 -0.791 vs. 0.605 -0.62), and a combined NM model incorporating limbic, associative, and sensorimotor subregional volumes further improved diagnostic accuracy (AUC: 0.833). Our fi ndings suggest that spatially heterogeneous degeneration within the SNpc provides complementary diagnostic information and that NM segmentation into distinct functional subregions may enhance the sensitivity of early PD detection. Furthermore, the inclusion of an independent external cohort demonstrated consistent diagnostic patterns across datasets, supporting the robustness and generalizability of NMbased biomarkers in early PD.

Previous studies involving patients with PD across a broad range of disease stages have consistently reported increased FW in the SN 10,13,18 . Similar FW elevations have been observed in early-stage PD cohorts, although fi ndings have varied in terms of anatomical speci fi city 11,19,20 . Zhou et al. 11 reported FW increases in the posterior SN but not in the anterior region, whereas Chen et al. 19 observed FW elevations in both the anterior and posterior regions. However, we did not observe a signi fi cant increase in the FW content within the SN of patients with early PD compared to controls. Several factors may explain this discrepancy. First, our cohort was restricted to early-stage PD (Hoehn and Yahr ≤ 2.5, disease duration ≤ 60 months), whereas many previous studies included patients across a

broader disease spectrum with more advanced stages. Because FW changes maybe more pronounced in advanced PD stages where neurodegeneration is more extensive, the early disease stage may have limited the detection of signi fi cant differences. Second, methodological differences in diffusion acquisition and processing may have contributed to these discrepant fi ndings. Our study employed single-shell DTI data (b = 1000 s/mm²) with 45 diffusion directions, whereas other studies utilized varying acquisition protocols across sites (20 -64 directions, different scanner manufacturers), whichmayhaveaffectedtheFWmeasurementsensitivity.Third,theuseofa high-resolution 7T-derived atlas with small, functionally de fi ned SNpc subregions may reduce effect sizes relative to larger or voxel-based regions of interest. Notably, this atlas-based segmentation was applied identically to NM and FW; the persistence of robust NM differences across subregions and in the external cohort argues against segmentation-related bias as a primary explanation.

An additional methodological consideration is the use of single-shell diffusion imaging re fl ecting routine clinical protocols. FW estimation from single-shell data is inherently ill-posed and requires stronger regularization than multi-shell approaches, which may reduce sensitivity to subtle extracellular alterations. Consequently, single-shell FW maps are more susceptible to noise and partial-volume effects and tend to yield conservative estimates. Although prior work suggests that single-shell FW maps can provide reasonable contrast under certain conditions 21 , the absence of FW group differences and FW -clinical correlations after multiple-comparison correction in our cohort likely re fl ects modest biological effect sizes that fall below the detectability threshold of clinically routine diffusion imaging.

Our fi ndings of reduced NM volume in the SNpc in early-stage PD align with those of prior studies that reported similar results in both early and mixed PD populations. Several previous investigations have consistently demonstrated reduced NM signal or volume in PD, with reported AUCs ranging from 0.83 to 0.93, supporting its diagnostic utility 16,17,22 -25 . The spatiotemporal pattern of NM loss observed in our study showed the most severe loss in the sensorimotor region (75% reduction), followed by associative (41% reduction) and limbic territories (29% reduction). This pattern is consistent with the longitudinal study by Biondetti et al., which demonstrated that NM changes follow the same spatial progression as dopaminergic dysfunction, beginning in the posterolateral sensorimotor SNpc and subsequently involving the anteromedial associative and limbic regions 26 .

In our study, the correlations between FW measurements and clinical motor scales did not remain signi fi cant after FDR correction. Previous studies have yielded heterogeneous results; some have reported positive correlations between FW and motor scores in patients with middle-toadvanced-stage PD 18,19 or in broader cohorts, including those with other parkinsonian syndromes 27 , whereas others have found no signi fi cant correlation between FW and UPDRS III 11,28 . Similarly, NM volume showed no signi fi cant correlations with motor severity after FDR correction, which contrasts with previous studies reporting a negative correlation between NM volume and motor impairment 16,17 . The absence of robust imagingclinical correlations in our strictly early-stage cohort is biologically plausible. NMdecline re fl ects cumulative dopaminergic neuronal loss and may precede measurable motor worsening, which typically shows limited variability in the earliest disease phases. Additionally, early PD is characterized by relatively narrow ranges of both motor severity and structural degeneration, reducing the likelihood of detecting strong cross-sectional relationships within this clinical window.

The higher diagnostic performance of NM compared to that of FW observed in our study may be attributed to differences in the underlying pathological processes. NM-MRI re fl ects the core neuropathology of PD, the progressive loss of dopaminergic neurons within the SNpc, through the visualization of NM, an intracellular pigment that decreases with neuronal death. FW imaging primarily re fl ects secondary neurodegenerative processes, such as extracellular space expansion resulting from axonal demyelination, neuroin fl ammation, and tissue degeneration -changes that typically become more pronounced as the disease progresses 29,30 . This biological distinction provides a plausible explanation for the consistently higher diagnostic performance of NM-based measures in early PD, suggesting that NM-MRImayhavegreaterutilitythanFWimagingindetecting early neuronal changes. Taken together, the harmonized evaluation of NM and FWimagingsupports the concept that dopaminergic neuronal loss and extracellular microstructural change may evolve along partially distinct temporal trajectories in early PD.

Our multivariable regression analyses demonstrated distinct patterns of demographic and disease-related in fl uences on the two imaging modalities. NM volume showed consistent associations with disease duration across all subregions, whereas neither age nor sex exerted meaningful effects except for a modest sex-related difference in the associative region, indicating that NMdecline largely re fl ects PD-related nigral degeneration rather than demographic variation. In contrast, FW measures were strongly in fl uenced by both age and sex in nearly all subregions, and disease duration contributed only minimally, with a signi fi cant association observed solely in the sensorimotor region. This pattern suggests that FW increases are driven predominantly by demographic and microstructural factors rather than by early-stage PD pathology, supporting the interpretation that NM-MRI provides a more speci fi c marker of dopaminergic neuronal loss, whereas FWimaging is more susceptible to non-disease confounders.

Recent biologically grounded frameworks for PD, such as the SynNeurGe research diagnostic criteria and the Neuronal alpha-Synuclein Disease biological de fi nition and Integrated Staging System (NSD-ISS) staging system, highlight the need to anchor diagnostic and progression models in validated markers of synuclein pathology and dopaminergic neurodegeneration 31,32 . Within these frameworks, MRI-based measures -including NM-MRI and FW imaging -are currently regarded as investigational markers of neurodegeneration and are not incorporated into the core diagnostic or staging structure. By providing a standardized, early-stage comparison of NM-MRI and FW imaging with validation in an independent cohort, our study offers empirical data that may help clarify how these MRImodalities can be positioned relative to the biological axes emphasized in SynNeurGe and NSD-ISS. The stronger diagnostic performance of NMMRI observed in our cohort is consistent with its closer linkage to dopaminergic neuronal integrity, whereas the more limited discriminative performance of FW imaging likely re fl ects its sensitivity to extracellular microstructural alterations that may be less prominent in the earliest stages of disease. These fi ndings suggest that NM-MRI may hold promise as a complementary imaging tool in future multimodal frameworks.

Beyond diagnostic classi fi cation, NM-MRI may have broader clinical utility. Its sensitivity to early nigral degeneration suggests potential roles in early diagnostic evaluation, strati fi cation or enrichment of clinical trials focused on prodromal or early-stage PD, and future longitudinal monitoring of nigrostriatal integrity once validated in follow-up studies. Although FW imaging showed more limited discriminative ability in early disease, it may complement NM measures in later stages when extracellular changes become more prominent.

While our study focused on differentiating early PD from controls under rigorously controlled conditions, diagnostic challenges in routine clinical practice often involve distinguishing PD from atypical Parkinsonian syndromes or mixed-pathology presentations. Because these disorders may demonstrate overlapping or divergent patterns of nigral depigmentation and microstructural change, future research exploring the performance of NM-MRIandFWimaginginmoreclinically heterogeneous populations -including atypical Parkinsonism and copathologies -will be essential for determining their translational value in real-world diagnostic settings.

This study has several key strengths. First, by analyzing both NM-MRI and FW within a large, well-characterized early-stage PD cohort, we were able to directly compare their diagnostic performance under controlled clinical and technical conditions. Second, all image pre-processing steps were conducted using fully automated pipelines. This approach helps reduce inter-rater variability and potential bias arising from manual region delineation, thereby enhancing consistency across subjects. Recent comparative studies have demonstrated that automated atlas-based approaches

provide greater reproducibility and measurement robustness than manual tracing methods in NM imaging, supporting their use as a practical strategy to improve consistency in research settings 33 . Third, we segmented the SNpc into functionally relevant subregions, enabling region-speci fi c analysis that substantially improved diagnostic accuracy and provided insight into the spatial pattern of neurodegeneration in early PD. Finally, the inclusion of an independent external validation cohort from a separate institution extends the study to a multi-institutional setting and supports the robustness and generalizability of the main fi ndings across sites using comparable 3-T protocols.

Our study has some limitations. First, this is a cross-sectional study conducted exclusively on 3-T systems. The use of 3-T scanners in both the primary and external validation cohorts ensures methodological consistency but limits generalizability to lower fi eld strengths; because NM contrast and diffusion metrics differ at 1.5-T, the diagnostic performance observed here may not directly translate to such settings. In addition, the absence of longitudinal follow-up precludes assessment of temporal stability or progression-related changes in NM and FW measures. Future multicenter longitudinal studies spanning both 1.5-T and 3-T platforms will be needed to establish broader applicability. Second, the control groups in both the primary and external cohorts consisted of individuals undergoing MRI for benign clinical symptoms ratherthan healthy volunteers. Although these disease controls may introduce heterogeneity and potentially reduce sensitivity to subtle FW alterations, they also re fl ect the real-world diagnostic context in which early PD must be distinguished from common nondegenerative conditions, such as essential tremor. Third, our study did not directly assess the reproducibility or test-retest reliability of the imaging measurements. Although fully automated pipelines reduce inter-rater variability, inherent subject-level measurement error may still arise from residual atlas-to-subject registration inaccuracies, structural mismatch between the atlas and individual midbrain anatomy, partial-volume effects at boundary voxels, and scanner-dependent signal variation such as B1/B0 fi eld inhomogeneity. Additionally, intracranial volume normalization applied to NM volumes may propagate small segmentation inaccuracies, particularly given the small size of the SNpc relative to total intracranial volume; however, this is expected to increase random variability rather than introduce systematic bias. Fourth, PD diagnosis was based on clinical criteria, which inherently carries a risk of diagnostic misclassi fi cation; however, all PD patients additionally underwent 18 F-FP-CIT PET or DaTSCAN to con fi rm presynaptic dopaminergic de fi cit, thereby reducing the likelihood of including non-PD cases. In addition, although we restricted enrollment to patients with a disease duration of less than fi ve years to limit stage-related heterogeneity, some degree of variability in pathological progression may still remain. Fifth, there was a temporal mismatch between MRI acquisition and clinical motor assessment, with a median interval of approximately two months. Although motor symptoms progress relatively slowly in early PD, this timing gap may have introduced additional noise and attenuated the strength of imaging -clinical correlations.

In conclusion, SNpc volume on NM-MRI demonstrated superior diagnostic performance for detecting early dopaminergic neuronal loss in PD compared with FW metrics. NM-MRI may also serve as a structural marker of nigrostriatal integrity and complement emerging multimodal biomarker strategies, supporting participant strati fi cation or enrichment in disease-modifying trials. Prospective longitudinal studies are warranted to validate its clinical utility and to explore its potential role in disease monitoring.

## Methods

## Study population

This retrospective study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of the Samsung Medical Center (IRB No. SMC 2025-03-075-001), and the requirementforinformedconsentwaswaivedbasedonthestudydesign.All MRI sequences, including diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) and NM-MRI, were part of the routine clinical brain MRI protocol for patients with suspected Parkinsonism at our institution. This study retrospectively analyzed imaging and clinical data acquired during routine clinical care.

Consecutive patients diagnosed with PDwho underwent MRI between October 2021and May 2023 were retrospectively identi fi ed by searching the institutional database at a single tertiary medical center. All participants met the following criteria: (1) Age &gt; 18 years; (2) Diagnosis of PD based on the clinical diagnostic criteria of the Movement Disorders Society 34 by an experienced movement-disorder neurologist; (3) Early stage of disease as de fi ned by a Hoehn and Yahr scale ≤ 2.5 and disease duration ≤ 60 months; (4) Absence of other concomitant neurological or psychiatric disorders; (5) Nohistory of intracranial surgery or traumatic brain injury. Disease-control patients who underwent the same brain MRI protocol as those in the early PDgroup during the same period were included. These individuals initially underwent MRI to evaluate possible Parkinsonism but were ultimately diagnosed with non-Parkinsonian conditions by board-certi fi ed neurologists. Participants with a history of major neurological, psychiatric, or systemic diseases were excluded. Additional exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) inadequate MRI protocol, de fi ned as incomplete slice coverage in DTI, NM-MRI, or 3D T1-weighted sequences; and (2) image processing failure. Imaging processing failure was de fi ned as errors identi fi ed during quality control of automated brain extraction, registration, or segmentation steps.

Initially, 349 subjects were identi fi ed from our institutional database, and24subjectswereexcluded:3duetoinadequateMRIprotocoland21due to image processing failure (including three segmentation-related failures identi fi ed during quality control assessment). Finally, a total of 247 patients withearly PDand78controlswereincludedinthisstudy.TheseverityofPD was evaluated using the Movement Disorder Society-UPDRS Part III (MDS-UPDRSIII) 35 , and the Hoehn and Yahr scale 36 . All subsequent image processing and analysis were performed blinded to clinical diagnosis.

An independent external validation cohort was retrospectively identi fi ed from Asan Medical Center. Consecutive patients who underwent brain MRI, including DTI and NM-MRI for suspected Parkinsonism between January 2024 and December 2024 were screened for eligibility. The same inclusion and exclusion criteria applied to the primary cohort were used for the external cohort. A total of 110 subjects were identi fi ed; 40 subjects were excluded due to an inadequate MRI protocol, and one subject was excluded due to imaging processing failure. The fi nal external validation cohort consisted of 69 patients with early PD and 70 controls.

## FWImaging Processing

Anatomical 3D T1w images were preprocessed in the following order: (1) bias fi eld correction using the N4 algorithm implemented in Advanced Normalization Tools, (2) anterior commissure-posterior commissure alignment,(3) registration-based brain extraction, and (4) registration to the Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) template.

DTIdatawereinitially denoised using the Marchenko-Pastur principal component analysis algorithm, as previously described 38 . Brain extraction wasperformedusingthe b = 0 s/mm 2 image to generate the brain mask. ## NMImaging Processing

NM images acquired at echo times of 4.1 ms were analyzed. A Tukey window 40 was applied to all the images to reduce noise. The images were skull stripped using the BEaST algorithm 41 and segmented into a tissue probability map (TPM) using the CAT algorithm 42 . A Swin UNETR-based segmentation model 43 was applied to the T1w images to generate a brain mask composed of individual labels for the midbrain, pons, thalamus, third ventricle, and ventral diencephalon. To construct the segmentation model, 198 T1w images were collected from a domestic hospital and manually annotated by an experienced neurologist. The dataset was divided into 155 images for training and 35 images for validation. Bias fi eld correction was performedontheT1wandNMimagesusingtheN4algorithm 44 , employing a brain parenchyma mask derived from TPM. The brain masks generated fromthe T1wimages were rigidly transformed into NM image space. Image registration was performed using the SyN algorithm (version 2.6.0; https:// github.com/ANTsX/ANTs) 45 .

Af fi ne registration was performed to align individual brain masks with a template as previously described 46 . The NMimageswerethentransformed into a template space using the previously computed af fi ne transformation. To ensure registration accuracy, regions missing due to differences in scan angle and location between individual images and the template were excluded. Nonlinear registration was performed between individual images, excluding missing regions and corresponding templates. Masks of the periaqueductal gray (PAG), SNpc, and its subregions (associative, limbic, and sensorimotor), which were manually annotated on a template by an experienced neuroradiologist, were transformed into individual spaces using previously computed af fi ne and nonlinear transformations. Segmentation quality was assessed through visual inspection of the atlas-tosubject registrations, including veri fi cation of SNpc boundaries, midbrain alignment, and identi fi cation of any clear misregistration or missing anatomical regions. Subjects who did not pass this quality-control assessment wereclassi fi ed as having image-processing failure and excluded from further analysis.

## Statistical Analysis

Normality of the data was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test. Descriptive statistics were used to summarize the patient demographics and clinical characteristics. Continuous variables were expressed as mean ± standard deviation for normally distributed data or median (interquartile range) for non-normally distributed data and were compared between patients with early PD and controls using independent t-tests or Mann -Whitney U tests, respectively. Categorical variables are presented as frequencies and percentages and were analyzed using the chi-square test or Fisher ' s exact test when appropriate.

The discriminative powers of the FW and NM biomarkers were evaluated using receiver operating characteristic curve analysis for the total SNandeachSNsubregion (sensorimotor, associative, and limbic). The area under the curve (AUC) values with 95% con fi dence intervals (CIs) were calculated. The optimal cut-off values were determined by maximizing Youden ' s index, and the sensitivity, speci fi city, accuracy, positive predictive value, and negative predictive value were calculated.
