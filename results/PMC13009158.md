## Cerebrospinal fluid and plasma metabolites in Parkinson's disease: a Mendelian randomization study OPEN

Jia-Li Wang 1,3 , Qianhui Zhao 2,3 , Ran Zheng 1 , Yi Fang 1 , Jin Cao 1  &amp; Bao-Rong Zhang 1 

Metabolites in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) and plasma have been implicated in Parkinson's disease (PD), but their causal roles remain unclear. This study aims to investigate the causal relationships between specific CSF and plasma metabolites and PD risk, using Mendelian Randomization (MR) analysis. We utilized MR with inverse-variance weighting (IVW) as the primary analytical method, supplemented by four additional MR models to validate robustness. Data included 1,400 plasma metabolites from the Canadian Longitudinal Study on Aging, and 338 CSF metabolites from the Wisconsin Alzheimer's Disease Research Center and Wisconsin Registry for Alzheimer's Prevention, USA. metabolites from the Canadian Longitudinal Study on Aging. PD outcome data were obtained from a GWAS metaanalysis by the International Parkinson's Disease Genomics Consortium. MR analysis identified six CSF metabolites with suggestive causal relationships with PD. Dimethylglycine, gluconate, oxalate (ethanedioate), and an unknown metabolite (X-12015) were positively associated with PD risk, while (1-enyl-palmitoyl)-2-arachidonoyl-GPC (P-16:0/20:4) and an unknown compound (X-23587) were negatively associated. In plasma, 49 metabolites demonstrated suggestive causal relationships with PD risk. Key metabolites included hydroxy-3-carboxy-4-methyl-5-propyl-2-furanpropanoic acid (hydroxy-CMPF), carnitine C14, and 1-dihomo-linolenylglycerol (20:3) with positive associations, and tryptophan, succinate to acetoacetate ratio, and O-sulfo-L-tyrosine with negative associations. Notably, O-sulfo-L-tyrosine emerged as the most significant protective metabolite in plasma, showing robust associations across four MR models. This study highlights causal relationships between specific CSF and plasma metabolites and PD risk, underscoring O-sulfo-L-tyrosine as a potential biomarker and therapeutic target. These findings provide a foundation for further exploration of metabolic pathways in PD pathogenesis and intervention strategies.

Keywords Parkinson's disease, Metabolomics, Mendelian randomization, Cerebrospinal fluid metabolites, Plasma metabolites

Parkinson's  disease  (PD)  is  a  progressive  neurodegenerative  disorder  primarily  characterized  by  the  loss  of dopaminergic  neurons  in  the  substantia  nigra,  leading  to  symptoms  such  as  tremors,  bradykinesia,  and rigidity 1,2 . Affecting millions globally, the prevalence of PD increases with age 3 . Despite extensive research, the exact etiology of PD remains unclear, involving a complex interplay of genetic, environmental, and lifestyle factors 4,5 .

Metabolomics,  the  comprehensive  study  of  small  molecule  metabolites  within  biological  systems,  has emerged  as  a  crucial  tool  in  understanding  PD 6 .  This  approach  allows  for  the  identification  of  metabolic changes  that  occur  in  response  to  disease  processes,  offering  potential  biomarkers  for  early  diagnosis  and targets for therapeutic intervention. Recent studies have underscored significant metabolic disruptions in both cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) and plasma of PD patients, pointing to systemic metabolic dysfunction associated with the disease 7 .  Analyses of plasma metabolomics have revealed alterations in various metabolic pathways, including those related to amino acids, lipids, and energy metabolism 8,9 . Similarly, CSF metabolomics studies have identified changes in neurotransmitter metabolites, oxidative stress markers, and other pathways specific to  the  central  nervous system 10-12 .  Given that CSF directly reflects biochemical alterations within the brain, while plasma captures systemic metabolic changes, analyzing both biofluids together provides complementary

Mendelian Randomization (MR) is a robust analytical method that uses genetic variants as instrumental variables (IVs) to infer causal relationships between exposures (e.g., metabolite levels) and outcomes (e.g., PD). This  method  addresses  confounding  factors  and  reverse  causation  issues  common  in  observational  studies, providing more reliable evidence for causality. Applying MR to metabolomic data in PD can identify causal metabolic pathways and potential biomarkers, advancing our understanding of PD pathogenesis and aiding in the development of targeted therapeutic strategies and more effective clinical interventions 14 .

## Patients and methods Exposure data

In  this  study,  we  utilized  plasma  metabolite  data  from  8,299  participants  aged  45-85  from  the  Canadian Longitudinal Study on Aging, which primarily includes individuals of European ancestry. The data included summary statistics for 1,091 blood metabolites and 309 metabolite ratios obtained via genome-wide association studies (GWAS) 15 . Additionally, the GWAS summary data for CSF metabolites were sourced from a study by Panyard et al., which included 338 metabolites in the CSF of 291 participants 16 . These data were derived from participants enrolled in the Wisconsin Alzheimer's Disease Research Center, with a mean age of 64.7 years, and the Wisconsin Registry for Alzheimer's Prevention, with a mean age of 62.0 years; all participants were of European ancestry.

## Outcome data

The outcome data for PD were obtained from a recent GWAS meta-analysis conducted by Nalls et al. 17 . This meta-analysis, performed by the International Parkinson's Disease Genomics Consortium in collaboration with 23andMe, included data from 16 cohorts with a total of 33,674 individuals: 15,056 PD patients and 18,618 control subjects. The GWAS primarily focused on identifying genetic variants associated with PD risk and adjusted for key covariates such as sex and age to ensure robust results. All individuals included in the meta-analysis were of European ancestry to minimize population stratification bias. It is important to note that the 23andMe data included in the meta-analysis was not publicly accessible, and therefore, this cohort was omitted from our analysis. This ensures that our study is based only on datasets that are publicly available and fully replicable.

## Instrument selection

To  ensure  the  validity  of  the  genetic  instrumental  variables,  we  conducted  a  rigorous  multi-step  selection process for single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), as detailed in Fig. 1. First, considering that genome-wide significance thresholds may be too stringent for CSF and plasma metabolites, we adopted a relatively relaxed threshold ( P &lt; 5.00E-5) to identify instrumental variables (IVs) for plasma metabolites 18,19 . This threshold aligns with those used in similar metabolomics studies, aiming to enhance detection sensitivity while maintaining the number and representativeness of genetic instruments. Second, to ensure the independence of selected SNPs, we applied clumping based on the European samples from the 1000 Genomes Project linkage disequilibrium (LD) reference panel, excluding SNPs with R 2 &gt; 0.001 within a 10 kb window. This process reduces potential genetic correlations and ensures the random allocation of alleles. Specifically, clumping groups highly correlated SNPs within an LD region and retains only the most significant SNP , preserving the independence of the instrumental variables.

Third, we harmonized the data to ensure consistent effect estimates, excluding SNPs with ambiguous alleles. For palindromic SNPs (e.g., A/T or G/C), we set the maximum minor allele frequency threshold at 0.3 to avoid orientation ambiguity. Fourth, we calculated F-statistics for each SNP and excluded those with F &lt; 10 to avoid weak instrument bias. The calculation of F-statistics, as referenced in 20 , evaluates the strength of the association between the instrumental variable and the exposure, with F &lt; 10 indicating weaker instruments that could compromise the reliability of MR analysis. Finally, MR analysis was conducted only on metabolites with more than  two  qualified  SNPs  to  ensure  robust  statistical  power.  These  steps  collectively  ensure  the  validity  and reliability of the instrumental variables used in the MR analysis.

## Mendelian randomization assumptions

MR was used to evaluate the causal relationship between exposure (plasma and CSF metabolites) and disease outcome (PD). MR analysis has three core assumptions: (1) SNPs are closely related to exposure, (2) SNPs are not associated with confounding factors, and (3) SNPs have no other (pleiotropic) way of affecting the results except through exposure 21 .

## Statistical analysis

We conducted MR analyses using the TwoSampleMR package 22  in R version 4.4.0. For the primary analysis, the inverse variance-weighted (IVW) method was employed to evaluate MR estimates, supplemented by four additional  MR  methods:  MR-Egger,  weighted  median,  simple  mode,  and  weighted  mode.  The  MR  results were expressed as odds ratios (ORs) with corresponding 95% confidence intervals (CIs). The IVW method is  considered  robust  for  estimating  causal  effects,  provided  that  genetic  variations  comply  with  the  three instrumental variable assumptions and are not influenced by pleiotropy. To account for multiple testing, we applied Bonferroni and false discovery rate (FDR) corrections.

## Reverse MR analysis

To ensure that reverse causality did not bias the findings, a reverse MR analysis was conducted for the metabolites that  showed significant associations with PD risk in the primary (forward) MR analysis. In the reverse MR analysis,  the  IVW  method  was  again  utilized,  maintaining  consistency  with  the  primary  analysis.  However, due to the availability of a larger number of instrumental variables for the reverse analysis, we applied a more stringent significance threshold ( P &lt;  5.00E-8) to select the genetic instruments. Apart from this adjustment, all other analytical methods and sensitivity tests were consistent with those employed in the forward MR analysis. By performing the reverse MR analysis, we aimed to confirm the directionality of the observed causal relationships and further ensure the robustness and reliability of our findings.

## Discussion Key findings from MR analysis

The findings from our MR study provide robust evidence for the metabolic underpinnings of PD. By leveraging genetic variants as instrumental variables, we inferred causal relationships between specific CSF and plasma metabolites and PD risk. Our MR analysis identified 49 metabolites with suggestive causal relationships with PD risk: 21 positively associated, 23 negatively associated, and 5 unknown compounds. In the context of CSF metabolites, we identified six with suggestive causal relationships to PD: dimethylglycine, gluconate, oxalate (ethanedioate), and the unknown metabolite X-12,015 were positively associated with PD risk, whereas (1-enylpalmitoyl)-2-arachidonoyl-GPC (P-16:0/20:4) and the unknown metabolite X-23,587 were negatively associated. Notably, in the plasma metabolites, we identified hydroxy-3-carboxy-4-methyl-5-propyl-2-furanpropanoic acid (hydroxy-CMPF),  carnitine  C14,  and  1-dihomo-linolenylglycerol  (20:3)  as  having  the  positive  associations with  PD  risk,  while  metabolites  such  as  tryptophan,  succinate  to  acetoacetate  ratio,  and  O-sulfo-L-tyrosine were found to be negatively associated with PD risk. Among these, O-sulfo-L-tyrosine stood out as the most prominent protective metabolite in plasma, demonstrating consistent and significant results across four different MR models, underscoring its potential role as a key metabolic marker in PD pathophysiology.

## Insights into tryptophan and mitochondrial metabolism

These  findings  highlight  the  multifaceted  metabolic  alterations  in  PD.  Our  study  found  that  tryptophan  is negatively associated with PD risk. This amino acid is not only crucial for protein synthesis but also serves as a precursor to serotonin, a neurotransmitter that regulates numerous functions in the central nervous system. In PD patients, serotonin system dysfunction is associated with non-motor symptoms such as depression and sleep disturbances 24 . Additionally, tryptophan metabolites possess antioxidative properties, reducing oxidative stress by decreasing the production of free radicals and enhancing the activity of antioxidant enzymes, thereby protecting  neurons 25 .  The  kynurenine  pathway  of  tryptophan  metabolism  also  generates  anti-inflammatory metabolites, which is important given the role of inflammation in PD pathology 26 .

Our study found that a lower succinate to acetoacetate ratio is associated with reduced PD risk. Previous research has shown that this ratio reflects the metabolic state of cells, particularly mitochondrial function, which is often compromised in PD. Mitochondrial dysfunction leads to disrupted energy metabolism, a hallmark of PD 27 .  An increase in succinate may indicate a block in the tricarboxylic acid cycle, contributing to oxidative stress by generating reactive oxygen species. Conversely, an increase in acetoacetate suggests enhanced ketone body metabolism, which can serve as an alternative energy source when mitochondrial function is impaired, thereby reducing oxidative stress by decreasing reactive oxygen species production 28 .

## The protective role of O-sulfo-L-tyrosine

Among  the  identified  metabolites,  O-sulfo-L-tyrosine  emerged  as  the  most  significant  protective  factor  in plasma, showing consistent and robust associations across four MR models (IVW , MR-Egger, weighted median, and weighted mode). While this relationship was not observed in our CSF data, its protective role is strongly supported by findings in other studies using larger integrated CSF datasets 29 . These results underscore O-sulfoL-tyrosine's potential as a metabolic biomarker and provide new insights into its role in PD pathophysiology. Mechanistically,  O-sulfo-L-tyrosine is  generated  from  tyrosine  through  sulfation  catalyzed  by  tyrosylprotein sulfotransferases,  and  its  circulating  concentration  is  strongly  influenced  by  the  availability  of  tyrosine  as  a substrate 30 . Therefore, lower O-sulfo-L-tyrosine levels in PD may primarily reflect reduced tyrosine pools, rather than changes in sulfation activity. This interpretation is consistent with previous reports of reduced tyrosine and phenylalanine levels and a decreased tyrosine/phenylalanine ratio in PD patients 31 , as well as emerging evidence that  phenylalanine  may  exert  protective  effects 32 .  Given  that  tyrosine  is  the  direct  biochemical  precursor  of L-DOPA and dopamine, diminished tyrosine availability could compromise dopaminergic neurotransmission, with O-sulfo-L-tyrosine serving as a proxy marker of aromatic amino acid sufficiency. Thus, our MR findings support a protective association of O-sulfo-L-tyrosine with PD.

## Lipid metabolism-related plasma markers

Notably, among the plasma metabolites, carnitine C14, 1-dihomo-linolenylglycerol (20:3), and 1-linoleoyl-GPG (18:2) demonstrated positive associations with PD risk. Carnitine C14 is a long-chain acylcarnitine and a marker of impaired mitochondrial fatty acid β-oxidation. Its accumulation can disrupt mitochondrial energy metabolism

and promote inflammation via activation of NF-ĸB and MAPK pathways, contributing to neurodegenerative processes 33 . Likewise, 1-dihomo-linolenylglycerol (20:3) and 1-linoleoyl-GPG (18:2) are glycerolipid derivatives that may alter phospholipid composition and membrane dynamics. Such alterations can disrupt synaptic vesicle trafficking and facilitate α-syn aggregation, a hallmark of PD pathology 34 . Supporting this, plasma lipidomics studies in PD patients have consistently reported dysregulation of glycerolipids, including diacylglycerols and triacylglycerols, which may contribute to neuroinflammation and mitochondrial stress 35,36 . These mechanistic insights reinforce the plausibility of a causal role for these lipid-related metabolites in PD pathogenesis.

## Metabolic pathways associated with PD

Due to the limited number of metabolites detected in CSF, pathway analysis was performed only for plasma metabolites. Our metabolic pathway analysis identified 15 enriched pathways, with the urea cycle and ammonia recycling remaining statistically significant after FDR correction. These pathways emphasize critical disruptions in nitrogen metabolism, which may contribute to neurotoxicity and disease progression in PD. Other pathways, such  as  alanine  metabolism,  glutathione  metabolism,  and  phosphatidylcholine  biosynthesis,  also  revealed potential  metabolic  impairments  linked  to  oxidative  stress  and  mitochondrial  dysfunction.  Although  these pathways did not pass FDR correction, they provide valuable insights into broader systemic alterations associated with PD, warranting further investigation.

In this study, we identified several causal metabolites associated with PD risk, including urea, the alanine to asparagine ratio, the ADP to choline ratio, and the histidine to pyruvate ratio. Notably, urea, the final product of the urea cycle, reflects ammonia detoxification capacity, and its elevated levels have been reported in multiple brain  regions  of  PD  patients,  potentially  contributing  to  neurotoxicity,  cognitive  impairment,  and  motor dysfunction 37,38 .  Alterations  in  amino  acid-related  ratios,  such  as  the  alanine  to  asparagine  and  histidine  to pyruvate ratios, suggest disturbances in nitrogen metabolism and amino acid catabolism, which may exacerbate ammonia accumulation. Although the ADP to choline ratio is not directly involved in the urea cycle, ADP is crucial for cellular energy metabolism, and the urea cycle itself is an adenosine 5'-triphosphate (ATP)-dependent process. Thus, impaired energy metabolism may indirectly compromise urea synthesis and ammonia clearance. Collectively, these findings support the involvement of urea cycle dysregulation and impaired ammonia recycling in  PD pathogenesis, consistent with clinical evidence linking elevated blood urea nitrogen, proteinuria, and renal dysfunction to greater PD severity and faster disease progression 39 .

Our findings also align with and expand upon previous MR studies on CSF and plasma metabolites in PD. Notably, Liao et al. 40 identified oxalate in CSF as positively associated with PD risk, consistent with our results and reinforcing the role of metabolic toxicity. In contrast, other metabolites such as ribitol and lysine were not replicated, likely due to differences in outcome datasets or instrument selection. In plasma, we confirmed several associations reported by Zhang et al., particularly lipid-related metabolites such as carnitine derivatives and amino acid-related compounds including tryptophan 41 . Additionally, both studies pointed to mitochondrial and nitrogen metabolic dysfunction as key features of PD. Our pathway analysis uniquely identified the urea cycle and ammonia recycling as significantly enriched pathways, which were not reported in earlier plasma-based MR studies. By integrating CSF and plasma data, our study provides a more comprehensive view of metabolic disruptions in PD and highlights distinct metabolic signals captured through CSF profiling. Our study extends prior work with broader metabolite coverage and more robust MR analyses.

## Clinical implications and future directions

This study represents the first application of MR to investigate CSF and plasma metabolites in PD. By addressing confounding  factors  and  reverse  causation,  this  approach  provides  valuable  insights  into  the  metabolic dysregulation associated with PD, though it does not fully eliminate potential biases inherent to MR methods.

Certain limitations should be acknowledged. Firstly, our study population consisted primarily of individuals of  European  descent,  which  may  restrict  the  generalizability  of  our  findings  to  other  ethnic  groups.  Future research should include more diverse populations to validate these results across different genetic backgrounds. Secondly,  our  analysis  was  limited  to  metabolites  with  available  genetic  data,  potentially  overlooking  other relevant  metabolites  and  pathways  involved  in  PD.  Expanding  metabolomic  profiling  to  include  a  broader spectrum of metabolites could yield a more comprehensive understanding of the disease. Thirdly, although this study utilized CSF metabolite-QTL data from Panyard et al. and PD GWAS data from Nalls et al. (2019), more recent and larger-scale studies have since become available. Notably, a CSF metabolomics GWAS based on approximately 2,000 individuals and an updated PD GWAS identifying 59 additional risk loci may offer more comprehensive resources for future replication and refinement of our findings.

Additionally, we employed a relatively relaxed significance threshold ( P &lt; 5.00E-5) due to the limited number of SNPs, consistent with the methodological practices reported in similar metabolite-based MR studies 19 . For comparison, results using a stricter threshold ( P &lt; 5.00E-8) for plasma are reported in Supplementary Table S10. While this approach helps ensure sufficient SNP selection for analysis, it may increase the likelihood of false positives. Furthermore, we performed multiple testing corrections using both FDR and Bonferroni methods (Tables  S8  and  S9);  however,  as  in  other  similar  studies 18 ,  none  of  the  associations  in  our  study  remained significant after correction, likely due to the large number of metabolites tested. To address these limitations, future  studies  should  consider  adopting  stricter  statistical  thresholds  and  implementing  multiple  testing corrections to minimize the likelihood of false discoveries. Expanding the dataset, either by increasing sample sizes or integrating additional cohorts, would also enhance the statistical power and reliability of the results. These measures will not only improve the robustness of metabolomic MR studies but also ensure comparability with existing research methodologies.

In conclusion, our MR study identified 6 CSF metabolites and 49 plasma metabolites with suggestive causal relationships with PD. Of these, 23 plasma metabolites and 1 CSF metabolite were protective, while 21 plasma

metabolites and 3 CSF metabolites were risk factors. Notably, O-sulfo-L-tyrosine emerged as the most significant protective  metabolite  in  plasma,  highlighting  its  potential  as  a  diagnostic  biomarker  and  therapeutic  target. Our metabolic pathway analysis further revealed critical disruptions in nitrogen metabolism, particularly in the  urea  cycle  and  ammonia  recycling  pathways,  which  may  contribute  to  PD  pathogenesis.  These  findings lay a foundation for future research to validate and translate these metabolic insights into clinical applications, ultimately enhancing our understanding of PD and improving patient outcomes.
