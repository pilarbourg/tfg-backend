## Introduction

Parkinson's  disease  (PD)  is  a  common  neurodegenerative disease of the elderly, affecting about 41 per 100,000 people over the age of 40 and 1,900 per 100,000 people over the age of 80 [1]. The etiology of PD remains incompletely understood, with current consensus suggesting a multifactorial  pathogenesis  involving  complex  interactions  between  genetic  predisposition  and  environmental  exposures.  The  primary  pathological  features  of  PD include  degenerative  changes  in  nigral  dopaminergic neurons and the formation of residual neuronal cytoplasmic Lewy bodies, predominantly composed of misfolded α-synuclein (α-syn) [2]. Emerging  evidence suggests that  neuroinflammation  and  the  immune  system  may also play a significant role in the pathogenesis of PD. Of particular  importance  is  the  systemic  nature  of  inflammatory  immune  responses  and  persistent  neuroinflammation in the brain, indicating that immune activation in PD patients may originate peripherally or occur simultaneously in both the central and peripheral systems, ultimately  contributing  to  neurodegeneration  [3].  Clinical manifestations of PD vary across individuals and encompass both motor and non-motor symptoms (NMS) that may arise before or after disease onset [4]. Motor symptoms  characteristic  of  PD  include  bradykinesia,  myotonia, and resting tremors. In contrast, NMS comprises neurological and psychological disturbances (e.g., cognitive  disorders,  sleep  disturbances,  anxiety,  depression, and  apathy)  and  autonomic  dysfunctions  (e.g.,  weight loss, gastroparesis, constipation, bowel dysfunction, and urinary abnormalities). Of which constipation, is one of the most common and earliest NMS of PD, with a prevalence of up to 80% in PD patients, and is often used as an early clinical indicator of the disease [5].

The gut microbiota composition in PD patients differs significantly  from  that  of  healthy  individuals.  Research indicates an increase in the proportion of pathologically relevant  bacteria  and  a  decrease  in  beneficial  bacteria [6].  The  gut  microbiota  may  play  a  role  in  PD  development  via  the 'microbiota-gut-brain  axis'  (MGBA),  a bidirectional  signaling  system  [7].  Dysbiosis  of  the  gut microbiota  can  lead  to  varying  degrees  of  neuropsychiatric  disorders,  while  brain  dysfunction  can  disrupt gastrointestinal  dynamics,  secretion,  and  immune  function,  exacerbating  PD  symptoms.  As  a  crucial  regulator of metabolic homeostasis, the gut microbiota exerts many of its health and disease-related effects through its metabolites.  These  gut  microbiota-associated  metabolites,  often  subject  to  co-metabolism  by  the  host,  regulate  numerous physiological and pathological processes. Among the most extensively studied metabolite categories  are  tryptophan  metabolites,  bile  acids,  and  shortchain fatty acids (SCFAs) [8]. Of these, Trp metabolism is a key pathway, mediating MGBA communication and regulating  gastrointestinal  function  [9].  The  three  primary  pathways  of  Trp  metabolism  in  the  gastrointestinal  tract-the  indole  pathway,  the  kynurenine  pathway, and  the  serotonin  pathway-are  directly  or  indirectly regulated by the gut microbiota. These metabolites can  modulate  central  nervous  system  (CNS)  function through  the  MGBA  [10].  Notably,  certain  metabolites serve as ligands for the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AHR), thereby enhancing intestinal barrier integrity, attenuating peripheral inflammation, and regulating neuroinflammatory  processes implicated in PD pathogenesis [11]. This review highlights the role of gut microbiota in modulating  Trp  metabolism  and  its  impact  on  PD  progression. We also explore the potential of targeting gut microbiota and Trp metabolism as therapeutic strategies for PD.

## Gut microbiota and parkinson's disease Gut microbiota dysbiosis in PD patients

The gut microbiota, comprising bacteria, viruses, archaea,  fungi,  and  protozoa  residing  in  the  gut,  is diverse,  complex,  and  dynamically  evolving,  with  bacteria  accounting  for  approximately  99%  of  the  species. The composition of the gut microbiota is influenced by various  factors,  including  genetics,  diet,  medications, and environmental conditions. A healthy gut microbiota regulates nutrient metabolism, provides immunoprotective effects, and plays a role in the MGBA, whereas dysbiosis  may  contribute  to  neurological  disorders  such  as Alzheimer's  disease  (AD),  multiple  sclerosis  (MS),  and PD [12]. Numerous studies have identified significant differences in the gut microbiota of PD patients in terms of α-diversity  and/or  β-diversity.  Although  inconsistencies exist  across  studies,  certain  findings  have  been  consistently  replicated.  Most  commonly,  a  decrease  in Faecalibacterium , Roseburia , Prevotellaceae ,and an increase in Verrucomicrobiaceae , Bifdobacteriaceae , Enterococcus , Akkermansia , Enterobacteriaceae ,  and Lactobacillaceae have been observed [13-17]. Similar patterns have been reported in animal models of PD [18, 19].

Compared  to  fecal  microbiota  transplantation  (FMT) from  healthy human  donors,  transplantation of gut microbiota from PD patients exacerbated motor impairments and pathological features in α-Syn-overexpressing mice [20].  Additionally,  in  two  groups  of  PD  mice  with identical  genotypes  and  overexpression  of  α-syn,  the motor function of germ-free (GF) mice was significantly better than that of mice with intact gut microbiota. Notably,  both  motor  function  and  abnormal  α-syn  aggregation  improved  following  the  removal  of  gut  microbiota [21, 22]. This suggests that alterations in the gut microbiota represent a risk factor for PD. Communication between the gut and the brain occurs through various neural, endocrine, immune, and humoral networks [23]. As an essential amino acid, Trp cannot be synthesized by animal cells. Therefore, Trp and its metabolites in the gut rely entirely on dietary intake and production by the gut microbiota. In the gastrointestinal tract, Trp metabolism occurs  via  three  main  pathways  (Fig.  2):  (1)  the  indole pathway,  where  Trp  is  directly  converted  to  indole  and its  derivatives  by  the  gut  microbiota;  (2)  the  kynurenine pathway (KP), where Trp is converted to kynurenine (KYN) by immune cells, intestinal epithelial cells, or certain  gut  microbiota  through  the  action  of  indoleamine2,3-dioxygenase  (IDO)  and  tryptophan-2,3-dioxygenase (TDO); IDO1 is widely expressed in immune and nonimmune tissues and is activated by certain immune and inflammatory  stimuli.  IDO2  is  primarily  located  in  the liver and kidneys, and its catalytic activity is lower than that of IDO1 [29]. (3) the serotonin pathway, where Trp is  metabolized  into  5-HT  by  tryptophan  hydroxylase (TPH1) in enterochromaffin cells (ECCs). ## Indole pathway

In  the  colon,  tryptophan  is  directly  catabolized  and metabolized by the gut microbiota into indoles and their derivatives, including indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), indole3-acrylic  acid  (IA),  indole-3-lactic  acid  (ILA),  indole3-acetaldehyde (IAld), 3-methylindole, indoxyl-3-sulfate, and indole-3-propionic acid (IPA) [31].  Different  bacteria  produce  specific  indole  derivatives  by  encoding  distinct  tryptophanases.  For  instance, Clostridia produce IAA; Streptococcus  pepticus produces  IA; Lactobacillus produces IAld; Clostridium botulinum , Clostridium perfringens ,  and Anaerobacterium  sporogenes produce  IPA; Tridiumsporogenes and Ruminococcus produce  tryptamine;  and Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium produce ILA  [32-35].  Indole  is  the  most  abundant  microbial tryptophan metabolite, followed by IAA and IPA. Over 85 species of gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, including Escherichia  coli , Bacteroides ,  and Clostridium spp. ,  are  known  to  produce  indole  via  tryptophanase [36]. ## Kynurenine pathway

The  KP  initially  degrades  tryptophan  into  KYN  via  the key rate-limiting enzymes IDO1 and TDO. KYN is subsequently metabolized through the following pathways [38]: (1) conversion to kynurenic acid (KYNA) by kynurenine aminotransferase  (KAT);  (2)  catabolism  to  anthranilic acid (AA) by kynureninase (KYNU); and (3) conversion to  3-hydroxykynurenine  (3-HK)  by  kynurenine-3-monooxygenase (KMO). The 3-HK is further converted to 3-hydroxyanthranilic  acid  (3-HAA)  by  KYNU,  which  is then  catalyzed  by  3-hydroxyanthranilic  acid  3,4-dioxygenase  (3-HAAO)  to  form  quinolinic  acid  (QUIN)  and eventually  oxidized  nicotinamide  adenine  dinucleotide (NAD+). Among these metabolites, KYNA is neuroprotective,  while  the  others-3-HK,  3-HAA,  and  QUIN-are neurotoxic [39]. The KP is primarily activated in the liver, with  most  metabolites  originating  from  peripheral  tissues. Some tryptophan crosses the BBB in free form via the non-specific large neutral amino acid transporter and is  directly  metabolized in the brain [40]. However, with aging  or  BBB  injury,  lipid-soluble  substances  produced by peripheral metabolism can penetrate the BBB, influencing CNS KP metabolism [41].

## Serotonin pathway

Serotonin, also known as 5-HT, is primarily found in the gastrointestinal  tract,  spleen,  blood,  and  CNS,  with  the vast  majority  synthesized  and  distributed  within  ECCs, and  only  1-2%  located  in  the  CNS  [42].  In  the  CNS, 5-HT is predominantly stored in vesicles at the terminals of 5-HT neurons in the raphe nucleus of the brainstem. These neurons project efferent fibers to various regions, including the substantia nigra, striatum, pallidum, hypothalamus, thalamus, and cortex. Additionally, 5-HT fibers project to the ventricular membrane and leptomeninges, where they come into direct contact with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), potentially reaching other brain regions. As a neurotransmitter, 5-HT exerts its regulatory effects only when bound to 5-HT receptors (5-HT-R). Although the CNS contains a relatively small amount of 5-HT, it plays a  critical  role  in  regulating  sleep-wake  cycles,  feeding, pain perception, cognition, and emotion [43]. In the gut, Trp  undergoes  the  serotonin  pathway  and  is  converted to  5-HT  by  TPH1,  a  process  essential  for  maintaining normal  intestinal  motility  [44].  Although  5-HT  in  systemic  circulation  cannot  cross  the  BBB,  the  gut  microbiota can influence Trp availability to the brain, thereby modulating the 5-HT signaling pathway in the CNS. This modulation, in turn, affects gastrointestinal function via the MGBA.

## Indole pathway metabolites and parkinson's disease

Indoles  and  their  derivatives  are  critical  signaling  molecules in the MGBA, influencing brain function, behavior, and gut barrier integrity [45]. Variations in the indole and its derivatives can arise from differences in the predominant gut microbiota across individuals and disease states. As mentioned earlier, gut microbiota dysbiosis is observed in PD patients. While plasma tryptophan levels  do  not  differ  significantly  between  PD  patients  and their  family  members  under  identical  dietary  conditions,  indole  and  IPA  levels  are  significantly  elevated  in PD patients and are correlated with changes in the relative abundance of specific bacterial genera in the gut [46]. This alteration may result from structural differences in the gut microbiota of PD patients, leading to changes in tryptophan metabolism and its downstream metabolites. IPA has been shown to inhibit neuronal death by reducing endoplasmic reticulum stress and preventing abnormal  protein  aggregation  through  its  role  as  a  histone deacetylase inhibitor [47].

Increased intestinal permeability and the translocation of  bacteria  and  their  products  can  induce inflammation and oxidative stress in the gastrointestinal  tract,  initiating  α-syn  accumulation in the ENS and contributing to the pathophysiological progression of PD [48]. Neuroglial dysregulation  and  elevated  levels  of  pro-inflammatory factors have also been detected in colon biopsies of PD patients, with these neuroinflammatory products further compromising  the  integrity  of  the  intestinal  epithelial barrier.  Indoles  and  their  derivatives  play  a  crucial  role in protecting the functional intestinal barrier by regulating gut homeostasis. Research indicates that supplementation  with  tryptophan  and  its  derivatives  enhances  the expression  of  mucins  and  tight  junction  proteins  [49]. In tryptophan-deficient mice, intestinal barrier integrity was restored through supplementation with IPA and IAA [50].  Indole,  IPA,  and  IA  also  contribute  to  intramucosal  homeostasis  by  reducing  intestinal  permeability  via PXR and AHR-mediated mechanisms [45]. Additionally, indole  pathway  metabolites  may  act  as  AHR  agonists, modulating  neuroinflammation  and  further  influencing neurodegenerative processes. Considering that impaired intestinal barrier function and inflammation are triggers for  PD,  increasing  levels  of  indole  and  its  metabolites may effectively inhibit the development and progression of  PD  by  modulating  peripheral  and  central  inflammatory responses.

## Kynurenine pathway metabolites and parkinson's disease

The  KP  is  the  primary  route  for  tryptophan  metabolism. KYN, the central compound in this pathway, can be converted  into  either  the  neuroprotective  agent  KYNA or the neurotoxic agent QUIN. A disruption in the balance of these endogenous compounds is observed in PD and correlates with the severity of its symptoms [51]. In PD animal models, plasma tryptophan levels are significantly  reduced,  while  KYN  levels  are  elevated,  suggesting  that  KP  is  involved  in  the  disease's  pathophysiology and that increased activity of IDO1 and TDO may occur. IDO1  is  widely  regarded  as  the  predominant  enzyme catalyzing this process under inflammatory and/or stressful conditions [52]. Studies reveal that PD patients exhibit  lower  serum  tryptophan,  KYNA,  and  KYNA/ KYN  ratios  and  higher  QUIN/KYNA  ratios  compared to  controls.  Patients  with  advanced  PD  (Hoehn-Yahr grade &gt; 2) show even lower KYNA and KYNA/KYN ratios and  higher  QUIN  and  QUIN/KYNA  ratios  than  those with early-stage  PD.  These  metabolic  changes  are  independent  of  dopaminergic  drugs,  indicating  that  KYN metabolism  in  advanced  PD  shifts  toward  the  neurotoxic branch [53]. Similar metabolic alterations occur in the CNS and are localized to specific brain regions. PD patients have reduced KYNA levels in the striatum and CSF and increased QUIN levels in the striatum and cortex.  Higher  CSF  QUIN  levels  are  associated  with  more severe  symptoms  and  increased  excitotoxicity  [54].  The KYN/Trp ratio and 3-HK levels are significantly elevated in  the  putamen  nucleus,  frontal  cortex,  and  hippocampus  of  PD  patients.  Plasma  3-HK  is  closely  linked  to symptom  severity  and  disease  duration  in  PD  patients. Both QUIN and 3-HK contribute to neuroinflammation and  further  promote  the  development  of  PD  through mechanisms such as N-methyl-D-aspartic acid receptor (NMDAR),  lipid  peroxidation,  reactive  oxygen  species (ROS)  production,  and  increased  nitric  oxide  synthase levels  [53,  55].  Elevated  3-HK  and  QUIN/KYNA  ratios suggest  overactivation  of  the  neurotoxic  branch,  with KMO playing a similar role to IDO1 in this branch. Even in  healthy  conditions,  3-HK  and  3-HAA  can  generate significant  amounts  of  free  radicals,  leading  to  oxidative stress and mitochondrial damage, which can induce CNS disorders [56]. Overexpression of KMO and 3-HK in  inflammatory  environments  further  elevates  downstream  QUIN  levels,  resulting  in  severe  neurological damage. In PD animal models, increased KYNA levels in the  brain  prevent  nigrostriatal  dopamine  neurons  from QUIN-induced  excitotoxic  injury  by  antagonizing  the activation  of  excitatory  glutamate  receptors,  particularly  NMDAR  and  a7  nicotinic  acetylcholine  receptor (α7nAChR) activation [57]. Research has found that the activity of KAT-I and KAT-II is reduced in the plasma of patients  with  PD.  Similar  changes  were  observed  in  PD

animal models-decreased expression levels of KAT-I in the substantia nigra led to a decline in KYNA levels [58]. Additionally,  NAD + is  a  crucial  coenzyme  in  the  body, essential  for  the  production  of  adenosine  triphosphate (ATP). Normally, NAD + levels decline with age but significantly  increase  during  neuroinflammation.  In  PD, mitochondrial  dysfunction  increases  NAD + consumption, driving the KP toward the terminal pathway where QUIN is activated to NAD+, further depleting KYN and reducing  KYNA  synthesis  [59,  60].  When  the  levels  of neuroprotective  metabolites  are  insufficient  to  counteract  neurotoxic  products,  the  excessive  production  of neurotoxic substances leads to neuronal death and accelerates PD progression.

Studies  have  reported  an  association  between  the  KP and  the  MGBA  in  PD:  BRAAK  et  al.  discovered  Lewy bodies composed of α-syn in the ENS during autopsies of  early-stage  PD  patients,  suggesting  that  α-syn  may propagate  to  the  brain  via  vagal  nerve  fibers,  with  the ENS potentially being the initial site of α-syn aggregation [61]. This hypothesis is supported by the observation that gastrointestinal  symptoms,  such  as  constipation,  often precede motor symptoms in patients with PD. Research indicates that excessive QUIN, abnormally produced by the  KP,  can  form  metabolite  aggregates  that  promote α-syn  accumulation  and  exacerbate  neuronal  toxicity [62]. From the MGBA perspective, the KP may serve as a  link  between  α-syn  deposition  and  gut  microbiotadysbiosis triggered by aging or other pathological factors may activate  the  KP,  leading  to  the  production  of  toxic metabolites that cross the BBB and induce α-syn aggregation. As key KP metabolites, QUIN and KYNA may play immunomodulatory roles within the MGBA [63].

The gut microbiota can influence the degradation rate and metabolic fate of Trp in the KP. Under conditions of gut microbiota depletion, increased plasma Trp concentrations lead to a reduced Trp/KYN ratio, which may be associated with altered activity of IDO and TDO. Notably,  microbial  restoration  can  normalize  the  enzymatic activity  of  these  enzymes  [64].  Preliminary  studies  suggest that probiotics such as Bifidobacterium infantis and Lactobacillus  johnsonii may  modulate  host  KP  metabolism  by  suppressing  IDO,  though  the  precise  mechanisms require further investigation [65, 66]. This finding, however, cannot be generalized to the entire Bifidobacterium genus , as Bifidobacterium longum did not alter Trp metabolism or the KP in animal models [64]. Although distinct gut microbiota alterations have been observed in PD populations, the specific impact of individual bacterial taxa on key enzymes of the KP remains incompletely understood. Under physiological conditions, KYN exhibits  low  affinity  for  KYNU,  a  critical  enzyme  in  the  neurotoxic branch of KP metabolism, meaning flux through this pathway occurs primarily under elevated  KYN

concentrations.  Certain  gut  microbiota,  including Escherichia coli , Bacteroides , and Clostridium , harbor KYNUhomologous  genes  and  may  influence  host  metabolic pools by competing for Trp or secreting metabolites (e.g., indole  derivatives)  that  modulate  host  KYNU  expression,  though  the  exact  mechanisms remain unclear [32, 67,  68].  SCFAs,  microbial  metabolites,  may  epigenetically suppress KMO, reducing the accumulation of neurotoxic  metabolites  and  exerting  anti-inflammatory  and antioxidant effects. Faecalibacterium and Prevotellaceae are  major  SCFA  producers,  and  both  are  significantly reduced  in  the  gut  of  PD  patients.  Thus,  gut  dysbiosis may directly drive or indirectly influence the shift toward the neurotoxic branch of the KP in PD. Additionally, the gut  microbiota  can  modulate  microglial  activity,  affecting  the  production  of  the  neurotoxic  metabolite  QUIN, while  also  regulating  NMDAR  expression,  suggesting  a significant role in KP metabolism and neurodegenerative disease progression [29]. Unlike the relatively straightforward  endogenous  Trp  metabolism  in  animals,  bacterial Trp  metabolism  in  the  gut  is  highly  complex  and  governed by multiple regulatory mechanisms. Many bacterial strains harboring Trp-metabolizing enzymes remain unidentified, and further research is needed to elucidate the coordinated roles of different bacterial species in generating Trp-derived metabolites.

## Serotonin pathway metabolites and parkinson's disease

Studies have shown that 5-HT is closely linked to NMS associated with PD. The 5-HT system primarily involves seven receptor families with a total of 15 subtypes. The loss, reduction, or alteration of certain receptors or subtypes  significantly  impacts  PD-related  NMS,  including neuropsychiatric symptoms, cognitive impairments, and gastrointestinal motility disorders [69].

Animal  experiments  have  shown  that  5-HT  levels are  significantly  reduced  in  the  basal  ganglia,  thalamus, frontal  lobe,  cingulate  gyrus,  and  hippocampus  of  PD model  rats  [70].  Similarly,  the  concentration  of  5-HT in  the  plasma,  brain,  and  CSF  of  PD  patients  is  markedly  decreased  [71].  Necropsy  studies  have  revealed that  5-HT  levels  in  the  caudate  nucleus,  middle  frontal gyrus,  inferior  parietal  lobe,  and  visual  association  cortex are generally reduced in PD patients. Changes in the 5-HT  transporter  (serotonin  transporter,  SERT)  have also been observed, mirroring alterations in 5-HT levels [72]. These reductions may account for the prevalence of emotional disorders in PD patients. The decline in 5-HT levels in PD is likely linked to the loss of 5-HT neurons in  the  dorsal  raphe nucleus (DRN), as evidenced by the presence  of  Lewy  bodies,  neuronal  death,  increased 5-HT neuronal activity, and other changes in the raphe nuclei. Moreover, some reports suggest that DRN neuron loss is more pronounced in PD patients with depressive symptoms compared to non-depressed patients, indicating a closer relationship between 5-HT and depression in PD [73]. Additionally, anxiety-like behavior in PD model rats  has  been  associated  with  reduced  5-HT  levels  due to inhibition of 5-HT1A receptors in the central nucleus of the amygdala [74]. Working memory impairment has also been observed in PD model rats, while activation or inhibition  of  5-HT4  receptors  in  either  the  lateral  rein nucleus  or  the  dorsal  hippocampus  improved  working  memory.  This  effect  may  be  mediated  by  changes in  monoamine  transmitters  within  cognitively  relevant brain  regions  such  as  the  medial  prefrontal  cortex  and hippocampus [75, 76].

5-HT in the gastrointestinal tract is produced through the conversion of dietary tryptophan by TPH1 in ECCs. Acting  as  both  a  neurotransmitter  and  hormone,  5-HT regulates intestinal epithelial cell secretion, intestinal  motility,  and  mucosal  immune  responses  via  5-HT receptors expressed on intestinal epithelial cells, enteric neurons,  and  immune  cells  [77].  Consistent  with  findings in GF mice, antibiotic-treated mice exhibit reduced colonic  5-HT  levels,  decreased  TPH1  expression,  and prolonged  colonic  transit  time  [78],  suggesting  that  the gut microbiota plays a crucial role in regulating the 5-HT system.  Akhtar  et  al.  reported  that  GF  mice  had  fewer enteroendocrine  cell  subtypes,  including  L  cells  and ECCs,  compared  to  specific  pathogen-free  (SPF)  mice. Administration of Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron restored cell  numbers,  indicating  that  the  gut  microbiota  influences ECC development and contributes to maintaining healthy gut function [79]. Additionally, a study found that spore-forming bacteria from both mice and humans promote 5-HT biosynthesis in colonic ECCs. These bacteria supply 5-HT to the mucosa, tubular lumen, and circulating  platelets,  regulating  intestinal  motility  and  platelet function. Spore-forming  bacteria-induced  metabolites were  shown  to  increase  5-HT  levels  and  enhance  gut motility, highlighting the gut microbiota's critical role in regulating 5-HT synthesis, release, and intestinal motility  [80,  81].  Further studies revealed that Clostridia and Ruminococcus animus  metabolize  tryptophan  to  produce tryptamine. Animal experiments demonstrated that supplementation  of  GF  mice  with  tryptamine-producing Bacteroides  Thetaiotaomicron enhanced  intestinal motility  [82,  83]. AHR  is  a  ligand-dependent  transcription  factor  that resides  in  the  cytoplasm  as  part  of  a  complex  with chaperone  proteins  Hsp90,  XAP2,  p23,  and  Src  in  the absence of ligands.  Upon  binding  to  a  ligand,  the  complex  undergoes  conformational  changes  and  translocates  to  the  nucleus  [86].  AHR  is  widely  expressed  in immune cells, the gut, and the CNS, where it is detected in  antigen-presenting  cells,  T  cells,  intestinal  epithelial cells,  intraepithelial  lymphocytes, innate lymphoid cells, neurons, oligodendrocytes, monocytes/macrophages, astrocytes,  microglia,  and  brain  endothelial  cells. Notably,  AHR  mediated  peripheral  homeostasis exerts downstream effects on CNS-resident cells, thereby influencing brain inflammatory cascades. ## Therapeutic opportunities and future directions

Traditional anti-PD medications have limited therapeutic efficacy, are prone to drug resistance, and may exacerbate constipation  as  a  side  effect.  Structural  and  functional alterations  in  the  gut  microbiota,  along  with  dysregulation  of  tryptophan  metabolism,  significantly  impact  the progression  of  PD  and  the  quality  of  life  for  patients. Modulating the gut microbiota and tryptophan metabolism  offers  a  potential  approach  to  alleviating  motor symptoms and NMS, thereby improving the overall quality of life for PD patients. The relevant therapeutic strategies are based on three primary mechanisms: (1) dietary modifications  to  influence  tryptophan  intake,  indirectly affecting  the  levels  of  available  tryptophan  metabolites; (2) modulation of gut microbiota abundance to regulate tryptophan  metabolite  levels;  and  (3)  development  of drugs that act directly on the tryptophan metabolic pathway and AHR. The main therapeutic modalities are summarized in Table 1.

## Diet and its effects on tryptophan metabolism and PD

Tryptophan, as an essential amino acid, must be obtained through diet and is primarily found in protein-rich foods such as meat, eggs, milk and its products, legumes, and nuts [112]. The body's tissue stores of tryptophan are relatively low [113], and the recommended daily intake for adults  is  approximately  250-425  mg  [112].  Tryptophan metabolism is influenced by factors such as age, diet, comorbidities, and medications. While the effects of tryptophan intake on peripheral and central metabolism are not fully understood, studies suggest that a tryptophanrich  diet  may  hold  potential  benefits  for  neurological disorders.

Dietary patterns significantly influence the gut microbiota and microbiome, leading to a surge in clinical trials  investigating  the  impact  of  dietary  interventions  on PD prognosis. Studies indicate that adherence to a Mediterranean  diet  (MedDiet,  characterized  by  high  intake of  vegetables,  legumes,  fruits,  nuts,  olive  oil,  and  fish, with  limited  consumption  of  red  meat,  dairy,  and  saturated  fats)  increases  beneficial  bacterial  genera  such  as Faecalibacterium , Roseburia , Eubacterium , Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron , and  Prevotella .  These  bacteria  produce  SCFAs  with  anti-inflammatory  properties,  exerting  neuroprotective  effects  [114].  The  ongoing  PD-Diet (NCT06207136) trial aims to compare the feasibility and efficacy  of  an  18-month  MedDiet  intervention  versus  a standard diet in PD patients without dementia. Previous research  suggests  that  MedDiet  components  modulate the gut microbiota, reducing PD risk by 30% and mortality by 40% [115]. A cohort study found that middle-aged women adhering to a MedDiet had a significantly lower risk  of  developing  PD  later  in  life  [116].  Additionally, this diet al.leviates constipation and gut inflammation in PD patients [117]. However, high fish consumption may lead  to  protein-drug  interactions,  potentially  impairing levodopa  absorption  and  necessitating  dietary  timing adjustments. The ketogenic diet (KD), while effective for weight  loss,  markedly  reduces Bifidobacterium abundance  and  may  increase  cardiovascular  risk  [118].  The NCT05469997 trial  evaluates  the  effects  of  a  ketogenic diet (70-80% fat, 15-20% protein, &lt; 10% carbohydrates) on motor symptoms and mitochondrial function in PD. Current  evidence  suggests  that  β-hydroxybutyrate,  the primary ketone body in KD, mitigates dopaminergic neuron  loss  and  mitochondrial  dysfunction  while  reducing oxidative  stress  and  neuroinflammation,  thereby  slowing  PD  progression  [119].  Combined  levodopa  and  KD therapy  demonstrates  superior  efficacy  over  medication alone, significantly improving both motor and non-motor symptoms [120, 121]. However, the low protein content in KD may interfere with levodopa absorption and efficacy, implying that its benefits may partly stem from enhanced drug bioavailability rather than direct central effects. The NCT05437640 trial compares a protein-redistribution diet (PRD, total daily protein intake should be restricted to &lt; 0.8 g/(kg·d), with strict limitation of daytime  protein  consumption  to  7  g,  while  allocating  all remaining  planned  protein  to  the  evening  meal)  with  a balanced protein diet in PD patients, assessing effects on motor fluctuations and muscle health. Mechanistically,  PRD  may optimize  levodopa  absorption  by  minimizing  competition from large neutral amino acids (LNAAs), yet a single high-protein meal fails to sustain muscle protein synthesis  in  elderly  individuals, potentially exacerbating sarcopenia and muscle loss. Other dietary patterns, such as the MIND diet (a hybrid of Medi and DASH diets), emphasize  plant-based  foods,  leafy  greens,  and  berries  [123]. Observational  studies  associate  high  adherence  with  a delayed  PD  onset  in  women  by  up  to  17.4  years,  likely attributable to anti-inflammatory components (e.g., berries, leafy vegetables). Two studies reported reduced PD risk with MIND diet adherence, underscoring its potential neuroprotective role [124, 125].

Trp-deficient diets are known to induce gut microbiota dysbiosis  and  increase  systemic  inflammation  in  aged mice  [126].  Conversely,  in  aging  mice,  a  diet  containing 0.4% tryptophan increased the abundance of beneficial bacteria such as Akkermansia and elevated levels of tryptophan  indole  pathway  metabolites  (e.g.,  IAA,  IPA) in the colon. This diet al.so activated the AHR, enhanced intestinal mucosal  barrier function, and  significantly reduced neurodegeneration and neuroinflammation through  modulation  of  adenosine  5'-monophosphateactivated  protein  kinase  (AMPK)  and  NF-κB  pathways [94].  Similarly,  a  0.6%  tryptophan  diet  enhanced  tryptophan metabolism in the 5-HT and indole pathways in chronic  unpredictable  mild  stress  (CUMS)  mice,  suppressed inflammatory responses, improved brain energy metabolism, and markedly alleviated depression-like and anxiety-like behaviors [49]. While a high tryptophan diet increases levels of certain tryptophan metabolites, it also results  in  dose-dependent  increases  in  their  excretion [127,  128].  A  high  tryptophan  diet  (1.5%)  was  found  to induce KYNA production and inhibit dopamine release from  rat  astrocytes,  underscoring  the  complexity  of dietary  tryptophan  interventions  across  different  models [95, 129]. However, no data are currently available on CSF or brain tryptophan levels following dietary supplementation in humans.

Although high-protein diets are the primary source of tryptophan for humans, they do not necessarily increase brain  tryptophan  levels.  One  explanation  is  that  large amounts  of  tryptophan  absorbed  from  the  gut  stimulate  hepatic  TDO activity,  converting tryptophan in the liver and reducing its availability in the CNS [130]. Conversely,  high-carbohydrate  diets  normalize  gut  microbiota  imbalances  by  decreasing Bilophila , Alistipes ,  and Bacteroides ,  while  increasing  the  abundance  of Bifidobacterium , Ileibacterium , Turicibacter , and Blautia . Such  diets  ameliorate  1-methyl-4-phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine  (MPTP)-induced  dyskinesia  in  PD  mice, reduce  dopamine  neuron  damage  and  increase  brain tryptophan  availability  by  facilitating  insulin-stimulated uptake  of  branched-chain  amino  acids  into  muscles, thereby  increasing  the  serum  tryptophan-to-large  neutral  amino  acid  ratio  [96].  Additionally,  high  levels  of dietary  polyunsaturated  fatty  acids  have  been  shown to  increase  free  tryptophan  levels  and  correlate  negatively  with  plasma  3-HK  levels  and  the  KYN/Trp  ratio [131].  Exercise  further  enhances  tryptophan  uptake  by the brain by producing high levels of non-esterified fatty acids that competitively bind to albumin, freeing tryptophan and increasing its serum levels [132]. Studies have demonstrated  that  60  min  of  moderate-intensity  exercise significantly boosts peripheral 5-HT synthesis in the brain,  which  depends  on  the  availability  of  tryptophan. This  increase  in  5-HT  synthesis  may  contribute  to  the antidepressant  effects  of  exercise  in  the  elderly.  Moreover, exercise enhances gut microbiota diversity, restores the balance between beneficial and pathogenic bacterial communities,  reduces  intestinal  inflammation,  ameliorates emotional disorders, and delays disease progression [133].

## Targeting gut microbiota for PD management

Considering  that  the  gut  microbiota  efficiently  catabolizes  tryptophan,  targeting  the  gut  microbiota  could potentially alter tryptophan availability. Antibiotic application has been shown to significantly reduce gut microbiota diversity in mice, subsequently affecting tryptophan metabolite levels. Treatment with a cocktail of antibiotics,  including  ampicillin  (1  mg/ml),  vancomycin  (5  mg/ ml),  neomycin  (10  mg/ml),  metronidazole  (10  mg/ml), and  amphotericin  B  (0.1  mg/ml)  from  21  days  of  life, resulted in a decreased relative abundance of Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes and an increased abundance of Cyanobacteria and Proteobacteria in the intestinal tract of adult mice.  This  antibiotic-induced  gut  microbiota  deficiency led  to  alterations  in  tryptophan  metabolism  kinetics, evidenced  by  elevated  tryptophan  levels,  reduced  KYN levels,  increased  Trp/KYN  and  KYNA/KYN  ratios  in peripheral serum, and significant changes in 5-HIAA (a 5-HT metabolite) in brain regions associated with behavioral regulation in cerebral-gut disorders. These changes suggest  a  potential  contribution  to  the  pathogenesis  of anxiety and cognitive disorders [134]. In a porcine model, antibiotic use resulted in alterations to microbial communities and tryptophan metabolites in the ileum and feces,

with  decreased  serum  tryptophan  levels  and  increased indole concentrations observed [135-137]. Additionally, tryptophan metabolism in antibiotic-treated pigs favored the  KP  over  other  pathways  [138].  Although  recent studies  have  explored  the  potential  therapeutic  role  of antibiotics  in  PD,  the  specific  effects  of  antibiotics  on tryptophan metabolism remain unclear. The use of antibiotics in the elderly, especially in the context of a chronic disease like PD, poses a dual challenge. Therefore, robust evidence  demonstrating  that  the  benefits  outweigh  the risks is essential before antibiotics can be considered for clinical use in this population.

Probiotics  have  been  validated  as  adjuvants  in  the treatment of neurological disorders, including PD. Probiotics such as Lactobacillus spp. and Bifidobacterium spp. may  modulate  host  tryptophan  metabolism  by  inhibiting the KP [65, 66]. Oral administration of Lactobacillus johnsonii to rats resulted in decreased serum KYN levels, increased  5-HT  levels,  and  reduced  gut  IDO1  activity. Similar  trends  were  observed  in  humans  after  8  weeks of  oral Lactobacillus  johnsonii administration  [97].  A previous  study  demonstrated  that  administering Bifidobacterium  infantis to  rats  decreased  pro-inflammatory cytokine levels, increased plasma tryptophan and KYNA levels,  and reduced 5-HIAA concentrations in the frontal cortex and dopamine concentrations in the amygdala, thereby exerting antidepressant effects [136]. Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium are  also known to directly convert tryptophan to 5-HT [139], and some strains, such as Lactobacillus  casei  subsp.  casei  327 ,  can  indirectly  promote  colonic  5-HT  synthesis  by  increasing  the  expression  of  TPH1  [140].  Furthermore,  certain Lactobacillus species can degrade tryptophan into indole compounds, including IAld, ILA, and IAA [33, 141]. Inoculating mice with  three Lactobacillus strains  capable  of  metabolizing  tryptophan  or  treating  them  with  an  AHR  agonist was  shown  to  promote  AHR-dependent  signaling  of microbial  tryptophan  metabolism,  influencing  peripheral tryptophan availability [98]. Some probiotics reduce the  conversion  of  tryptophan  to  KP  metabolites  while enhancing the synthesis of indole pathway AHR ligands. To date, studies investigating the effects of probiotics on PD models through tryptophan metabolism are limited. However, mechanistically, probiotics may effectively alleviate PD symptoms. Prebiotics, substrates selectively utilized by host microorganisms that confer health benefits, primarily consist of oligosaccharides (composed of 2-10 monosaccharide molecules). Clinical and preclinical  studies  have  shown  that  inulin  and  galactooligosaccharides  increase  the  abundance  of  indole-producing probiotics  such  as Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus . However, this evidence is currently insufficient to establish prebiotic interventions as a standard tool in the clinical management of PD [142].

Studies  have  shown  that  FMT  significantly  alleviates gut  microbiota  dysbiosis  and  holds  promise  as  a  therapeutic  approach  for  neuropsychiatric  disorders.  It  has also  demonstrated  the  involvement  of  gut  microbiota dysbiosis in the pathogenesis of PD through the MGBA [143]. In a rotenone-induced mouse model of PD, FMT ameliorated gastrointestinal dysfunction and  motility deficits  by  modulating  gut  microbiota  dysbiosis.  This effect  was  achieved  by  inhibiting  gut  and  brain  inflammation mediated through the TLR4/MyD88/NF-κB signaling pathway [98]. Clinical data on FMT in PD patients remain  limited.  A  randomized,  placebo-controlled  trial involving  patients  with  mild-to-moderate  PD  (HoehnYahr grade 1-3) showed a significant increase in microbial diversity in the FMT-treated group compared to the placebo group, along with notable improvements in gastrointestinal dysfunction and cognitive function. No serious adverse events were reported during follow-up [99]. Improvements  in  motor  and  NMS  were  also  observed after FMT using conventional endoscopic routes such as colonoscopy,  naso-jejunum,  and  naso-duodenal  tubes. Additionally, greater efficacy and longer maintenance of therapeutic effects were noted in the colonic-type microbiota transplantation group compared to the mesenterictype  microbiota  transplantation  group  [100,  101].  The administration  of Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium via FMT has shown improvements in neurological and psychiatric symptoms, which may be mechanistically linked to an increased abundance of indole-producing microorganisms in the gut. This increase indirectly affects indole and its derivative levels and modulates inflammatory signaling pathways [144].

## Modulating Tryptophan metabolism and AHR as a therapeutic approach

Downstream neurotoxic metabolites of tryptophan contribute  to  neuroinflammation  and  the  progression  of PD, making them potential therapeutic targets. Enzyme inhibitors  and  KYNA  analogs  can  reduce  the  production  of  neurotoxic  branch  products  such  as  QUIN  and 3-HK, as well as the activation of excitatory amino acid receptors. This ensures that increased concentrations of KYNA in the CNS may effectively slow PD progression. Studies  have  shown  that  nicotinylalanine,  an  inhibitor  of  KMO  and  kynureninase,  increases  KYNA  levels, antagonizes  excitatory  NMDAR  activity,  and  reduces the  excitotoxicity  of  QUIN  and  3-HK  in  the  substantia  nigra pars compacta (SNpc) in neurological disorder models, including PD [102, 103, 145]. Additional enzyme inhibitors,  such  as  meta-nitro  benzoyl  alanine  (inhibiting kynurenine-3-hydroxylase) and ortho-methoxy benzoyl alanine (inhibiting kynureninase), may also theoretically reduce neurotoxic branch products and provide neuroprotective  effects  [104].  Preclinical  studies  have

found  that  Zonisamide  (ZNS)  increases  the  release  of KYN and  KYNA  from  cultured  astrocytes  and  inhibits TNFα-induced  QUIN  elevation.  Clinical  trials  demonstrated that ZNS, administered at lower doses than those used for epilepsy treatment, improved motor symptoms and  reduced  L-DOPA-induced  dyskinesia  (LIDs)  in  PD patients.  This  suggests  ZNS's  potential  as  an  adjunctive therapy  for  PD  during  L-DOPA  monotherapy,  achieved by  targeting  tryptophan  KP  metabolites  [105-107].  In vitro neurodegenerative disease models, N,N'-bis(2hydroxybenzyl)ethylenediamine-N, N'-diacetic acid (HBED) showed neuroprotective effects by inhibiting  the  activity  of  3-hydroxyanthranilic  acid  oxygenase (3-HAO),  the  enzyme  directly  responsible  for  QUIN biosynthesis  in  the  brain  [103].  Similarly,  the  inhibitor  Ro-61-8048  increased  levels  of  L-KYN  and  KYNA while  reducing  LIDs  without  affecting  the  anti-parkinsonian  effects  of  L-DOPA  [108].  Excitatory  neurotoxicity caused by heightened glutamatergic activity is closely associated  with  the  development  and  maintenance  of LIDs [145]. KYNA and its analogs may act as glutamate receptor antagonists to provide neuroprotection via the KP. Although the direct administration of KYNA shows therapeutic  promise,  its  clinical  application  is  limited by  poor  BBB  permeability.  Developing  KYNA  analogs, such as glucosamine-kynurenic acid, which can cross the BBB,  offers  a  potential  solution.  Once  inside  the  brain, KYNA binds to excitatory glutamate receptors, particularly  NMDAR,  to  exert  neuroprotective  effects  [102]. 7-chloro-kynurenic acid (7-Cl-KYNA) and its biological precursor  L-4-chlorokynurenine  (4-Cl-KYN)  have  been shown to inhibit QUIN neurotoxicity in the hippocampus and striatum in animal models [109, 110]. Inhibition of TDO and KMO has also reversed disease phenotypes in  Drosophila models of AD, PD, and Huntington's disease,  demonstrating  therapeutic  potential  for  neurodegenerative conditions [146].

Tryptophan metabolic pathway products, particularly  indoles  and  their  derivatives,  act  as  AHR  agonists, modulating inflammatory responses and cell physiology by  targeting  specific  cells  [147,  148].  Regulating  tryptophan  metabolism  to  promote  the  synthesis  of  AHR ligands  has  dual  benefits:  it  enhances  gut  barrier  function  and  mitigates  peripheral  inflammatory  responses while simultaneously binding to astrocytes in the CNS to reduce CNS inflammation. Probiotics such as Lactobacillus  reuteri have  been  shown to increase the production of AHR agonists indole-3-aldehyde, successfully reducing inflammation [149]. In MPTP-induced mouse models of PD, increased expression of AHR-positive microglia and astrocytes aggravated neuroinflammation in the striatum and SNpc. However, AHR activation in residual dopaminergic neurons in the SNpc appeared to have a neuroprotective effect [150]. Agonists acting directly on AHR can serve as substitutes for endogenous AHR ligands involved in the tryptophan AHR-mediated anti-inflammatory pathway.  For  instance,  the  AHR  ligand  diindolylmethane  prevented  dopaminergic  neuron  loss  in  the  striatum  and  SNpc  in  MPTP-induced  mouse  models  [151]. Additionally, the endogenous AHR ligand 2-(1'H-indole3'-carbonyl)-thiazole-4-carboxylic  acid  methyl  ester  has been shown in clinical studies to inhibit the progression of autoimmune encephalomyelitis through both injection and oral administration [152]. Targeting and modulating specific probiotics or administering synthetic analogs of AHR  agonists  optimized  for  anti-inflammatory  activity and  biodistribution  may  represent  promising  therapeutic strategies for non-specific neurological inflammation in  neurodegenerative  diseases.  However,  a  significant challenge  in  developing  AHR-targeted  therapeutics  lies in  the  precise  delivery  of  AHR  ligands  to  target  tissues and  cells  while  minimizing  non-specific  modulation  in other tissues. Nanoparticle-based targeted drug delivery may provide a viable solution in the future. Beyond AHRtargeted approaches, drugs affecting other targets within the tryptophan metabolic pathway may also hold therapeutic potential, warranting further exploration in future studies.

## Shortcomings and prospects

PD  is  a  neurodegenerative  disorder  mediated  by  multiple  mechanisms.  The  gut  microbiota  and  tryptophan metabolism  are  complex,  interacting  processes  influenced  by  genetic  backgrounds,  individual  differences, dietary patterns, co-morbidities, medications, infections, and  lifestyle  factors.  While  it  remains  unclear  whether dysregulation  of  gut  microbiota-associated  tryptophan metabolism  is  a  cause  or  consequence  of  PD,  its  association with the disease is well established. Most current studies  on  mechanisms  and  interventional  treatments have been based on preclinical and cross-sectional studies led to the current limitations of the evidence base. It is important to incorporate humanized mouse models to better  account  for  the  known  differences  in  tryptophan metabolism and AHR activity between human and mouse gut microbiota. Additionally, variations in gut microbiota due  to  differences  in  PD  staging,  co-morbidities,  BBB integrity,  and  dietary  and  environmental  factors  among individuals lead to disparities in tryptophan metabolites entering systemic circulation, particularly those crossing the BBB into the CNS. Therefore, the study of tryptophan metabolism  must  be  integrated  with  investigations  into the  composition  of  the  gut  microbiota.  Moreover,  the complex  properties  including  species,  cell  type,  tissuespecific,  and  ligand-specific  differences  complicate  the extrapolation of in vitro findings to in vivo contexts and the  translation  of  animal  model  observations  to  human studies.  Future  research  should  explore  differences  in

gut  microbiota-mediated  tryptophan  metabolism  and the  dynamic evolution of central,  peripheral,  and  intestinal  metabolic pathways in PD across clinical stages or with  specific  prominent  symptoms.  Investigations  into short-  and  long-term  metabolic  changes  following  specific  therapeutic  regimens  may  provide  novel  insights into the interactions between the gut microbiota and the physiological  and  pathological  processes  of  PD.  These efforts could guide the development of future treatments for inflammatory, neoplastic, and CNS degenerative diseases by addressing the intricate relationship between gut microbiota and disease progression.

## Conclusion

While  multiple  factors  are  known  to  contribute  to  the pathogenesis  of  neurodegenerative  disorders,  chronic neuroinflammation emerges as a pivotal mediator of neuronal  injury  and  degeneration.  In  PD,  the  dysregulated gut  microbiota  profile  may  potentiate  neuroinflammatory  responses  through  modulation  of  local  tryptophan metabolic pathways and AHR-dependent signaling mechanisms,  which  constitute  essential  immunomodulatory  axes  operating  at  both  the  intestinal  barrier  and central  nervous  system  interfaces.  The  metabolic  pathways of tryptophan in the body are directly or indirectly regulated  by  the  gut  microbiota.  The  gut  microbiota plays a critical role in mediating tryptophan metabolism within the MGBA, influencing gastrointestinal function. Some tryptophan metabolites act as ligands for the AHR, contributing to the enhancement of the intestinal mucosal  barrier,  suppression  of  peripheral  inflammation,  and modulation of CNS inflammation.

In  summary, our findings provide a novel perspective that  targeting  the  gut  microbiota  and  regulating  tryptophan  metabolism  may  offer  a  promising  therapeutic strategy  for  PD.  Interventions  such  as  specific  probiotics,  prebiotics, and FMT could effectively target the gut microbiota and modulate tryptophan metabolism. Alternatively, adopting specific dietary patterns, regulating key enzymes in the tryptophan metabolic pathway, or directly targeting AHR and its ligands may also represent viable treatment  approaches.  Future  research  should  focus  on further  elucidating  the  specific  roles  of  the  gut  microbiota  and  tryptophan  metabolism  in  the  pathogenesis of  PD.  Moreover,  understanding  how  to  precisely  regulate these factors to alleviate symptoms and improve the quality of life of PD patients will be critical for advancing therapeutic strategies.
