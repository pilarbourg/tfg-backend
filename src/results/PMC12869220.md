## Introduction

Overview of Parkinson's disease (PD) and the importance of multiorgan axis research

PD is one of the most prevalent neurodegenerative disorders globally, characterized by progressive degeneration of dopamine (DA) neurons in the nigrostriatal pathway of the midbrain [1-4]. Clinically, PD patients typically exhibit classic motor symptoms such as resting tremor and bradykinesia, accompanied by non-motor symptoms including sleep disorders and g astrointestinal dysfunction [5]. PD is increasingly recognized as a systemic disorder characterized by bidirectional interactions between the brain and peripheral organs, with recent advancements in multi-organ axis research transforming our understanding of its pathogenesis. A prominent theory, the ''enterogenic disease' hypothesis, proposes that PD may originate in the gut and later spread to the brain, supported by substantial evidence. First, early disturbances in the enteric nervous system (ENS) are observed: around 30 % of PD patients exhibit ENS abnormalities, such as reduced enteric neurons and gliosis, years before the onset of motor symptoms [6-8]. Second, pathological a -synuclein aggregates, a hallmark of PD, are found in the ENS of postmortem PD patients, suggesting retrograde propagation to the brain via the vagus nerve; this pathway is corroborated by animal models where intra-gastric administration of a -synuclein fi bri ls induces PD-like pathology in the brainstem and midbrain [9]. Additionally, gut microbiota dysbiosis is evident in PD patients, marked by decreased Prevotellaceae and increased Enterobacteriaceae [10]. These compositional shifts correlate with intestinal inflammation, heightened gut permeability (''leaky gut'), and systemic elevation of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., tumor necrosis factora (TNFa ), interleukin-6 (IL-6)), which exacerbate neuroinflammation and dopaminergic neurodegeneration [11]. Dietary interventions targeting the gut microbiota, including high-fiber diets and probiotics like Lactobacillus plantarum PS128, show promise in PD models by restoring microbial balance, reducing inflammation, and alleviating both motor and non-motor symptoms [12]. Collectively, these fi ndings underscore the gut-brain axis as a critical contributor to PD pathogenesis, highlighting the potential of gastrointestinal mechanisms in disease initiation and progression. Meanwhile, the liver, serving as the metabolic hub of the human body, is highly likely to impact the function of neuronal cells in the brain through certa in metabolites or signaling molecules, thus linking to the occurrence and development of PD [13]. Similarly, organs such as the heart, muscles, and bones may also form interactive ''axis' relationships with the brain, mediated by various mechanisms including neural, endocrine, and immune pathways. The interplay among these multi-organ axes collectively constitutes a complex pathophysiological network in PD. Therefore, in-depth exploration of the associations among the multi-organ axes (lungbrain axis, liver-brain axis, heart-brain axis, muscle-brain axis, bone-brain axis, and gut-brain axis) i n PD is crucial for comprehensively understanding the pathogenesis of PD and identifying more effective therapeutic strategies.

The potential role of Multi-Organ axes in the pathogenesis of PD

The etiology and pathogenesis of PD are not fully understood at present. It is widely accepted that PD is not caused by a single factor but rather results from the interplay of multiple factors, with age, environment, lifestyle, and genetic factors all contributing to its onset and progression [14]. In this process, multiple systems and mechanisms involved in multi-organ axes play potentially significant roles. Taking the gut-brain axis as an example, abnormal changes in gut microbiota can affect brain function through vari- ous pathways. In PD patients, there are abnormal elevations of pro-inflammatory cytokine genes, glial markers such as glial fi brillary acidic protein (GFAP), Sry-related HMg-Box gene 10 (SOX10), Soweto First 1000-Day Study (S1000), and decreased GFAP phosphorylation in the intestine, suggesting that intestinal inflammation and abnormal glial cell regulation may be related to the occurrence of PD [15]. Moreover, studies have shown [16] that dextran sulfate sodium-induced colitis can exacerbate lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced neuroinflammatory responses (such as neurodegeneration), manifested by increased mRNA transcription of TNFa , inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and interleukins (ILs) in brain tissue. This chronic intestinal inflammation may increase blood-brain barrier (BBB) permeability, activate and infiltrate immune cells, ultimately leading to abnormal inflammatory responses in the CNS and promoting neuronal apoptosis.

The lung is a crucial organ for gas exchange in the human body, and its condition influences brain function through bidirectional molecular pathways. Emerging evidence reveals that a -synuclein aggregates, the pathological hallmark of PD, have been detected in pulmonary neuroendocrine cells and alveolar macrophages. These aggregates may propagate via the vagus nerve to the dorsal motor nucleus of the vag us in the medulla, establishing a lung-brain axis in PD pathogenesis [17]. At the molecular level, PD-related neurotransmitter alterations (particularly dopamine depletion) impair pulmonary surfactant production through D1 receptor-mediated pathways [18], while excessive acetylcholine signaling may induce bronchoconstriction and mucus hypersecretion [19]. Pulmonary diseases such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) not only lead to hypoxemia but also exacerbate neuroinflammation through systemic circulation of pr o-inflammatory cytokines [20]. Specifically, TNFa activates TNFR1 receptors on cerebral endothelial cells, triggering nuclear factorj B (NFj B)-mediated transcription of adhesion molecules that facilitate leukocyte infiltration into the ce ntral nervous system (CNS) [21]. Concurrently, IL-6 induces neuronal apoptosis via Janus Kinase (JAK) / signal transducers and activators of transcription (STAT) signaling pathway activation, with synergistic effects observed when co-expressed with TNFa [22]. These mechanisms may explain the observed association between COPD and accelerated PD progression. The liver is involved in substance metabolism, and its impaired function may disrupt neurotransmitter balance through dysregulation of aromatic amino acid decarboxylase activity and altered dopamine sulfa -tion capacity, which is linked to PD symptoms [23]. The heart's pumping function and secreted neuroendocrine factors affect cerebral blood supply and neuromodulation, with cardiac abnormalities potentially impacting dopaminergic neurons via chronic cerebral hypoperfusion-induced oxidative stress in the substantia nigra pars compacta, pa rticipating in the pathogenesis of PD [24]. Muscle physiological function and signaling issues may influence brain neuronal activity through impaired myokine secretion (particularly brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and irisin) that normally support neuroplasticity, being related to the motor symptoms of PD [25]. Bone is not only a supportive structure but also interacts with the nervous system through receptor activator of nuclear factorj B ligand (RANKL)-osteoprotegerin (OPG) signaling pathway, and its internal cytokines may affect the brain microenvironment. PD patients are prone to osteoporosis, suggesting a potential interplay between these conditions [26]. These fi ndings emphasize PD as a systemic disorder, challenging traditional brainfocused paradigms and highlighting the necessity for therapies addressing multi-organ interactions (Fig. 1). ## Lung-Brain axis and PD

The potential role of the lung in PD

Patients with PD often exhibit abnormalities in lung function, which are correlated with disease progression. Multiple clinical studies have found that compared to healthy individuals, PD patients often show decreased vital capacity, forced vital capacity, and forced expiratory volume (FEV) in one second, among other indicators, reflecting impaired ventilatory function [27,28]. For example, pulmonary function tests conducted on eight PD patients, compared with an age- and gender-matched healthy control group, revealed varying degrees of expiratory flow limitation in the patient group, and as PD severity increased, abnormal lung function manifestations became more pronounced [29].

From a symptomatic perspective, many PD patients report dyspnea and shortness of breath, particularly prominent in the middle to late stages of the disease [30,31]. This may be related, on the one hand, to muscle rigidity affecting respiratory muscles, such as restricted movement of intercostal muscles and the diaphragm, impacting normal chest expansion and contraction as well as pulmonary ventilation. On the other hand, autonomic dysfunction caused by PD may indirectly interfere with normal physiological regulatory mechanisms of the lungs, leading to abnormalities in respiratory rhythm and depth.

Furthermore, the risk of pulmonary infections is significantly increased in PD patients [32,33]. Motor impairments due to the disease, such as dysphagia, can lead to aspiration of food or liquids into the trachea, triggering aspiration pneumoni a. Concurrently, factors like prolonged bed rest, reduced physical activity, and weakened immune function collectively make the lungs more sus- ceptible to pathogens like bacteria and viruses. Once a pulmonary infection occurs, it further exacerbates symptoms such as dyspnea in patients, affecting their overall condition and quality of life.

## Impact of PD on pulmonary function

Mechanism of motor impairments on respiratory function

The motor impairments characteristic of PD, particularly muscle rigidity and bradykinesia, exert a direct and significant influence on respiratory function. Muscle rigidity can affect respiratory muscles, with rib cage muscle stiffness limiting normal expansion and contraction movements of the thorax, resulting in reduced lung air intake per breath. Limited diaphragmatic mobility also prevents it from fully descending during inspiration, decreasing volumetric changes in the thorax and impacting pulmonary ventilation. For instance, during respiratory function tests on PD patients, a marked reduction in thoracic mobility compared to healthy individuals is observed, along with abnormal changes in respiratory rate and depth, all closely linked to respiratory muscle rigidity [34].

Bradykinesia cannot be overlooked either. Patients' slowed movements impair the efficiency of actions that facilitate lung ventilation and mucus expulsion, such as coughing and deep breathing. Normally, effective coughing requires coordinated and rapid contraction and relaxation of multiple muscle groups to generate sufficient airflow for expelling airway secretions. However, due to bradykinesia, PD patients cannot perform forceful and timely coughing, allowing mucus and other secretions to accumulate in the lungs, increasing the risk of pulmonary infections and further impeding normal gas exchange in the lungs [35,36].

Dopamine, as a crucial neurotransmitter, not only plays a role in regulating motor control in the brain but also affects the lungs [30]. Studies have found that the dopaminergic system is involved in the autonomic regulation of the lungs. Reduced dopamine levels in the brains of PD patients may indirectly interfere with the normal function of pulmonary autonomic nerves, leading to abnormal tension regulation of bronchial smooth muscles and affecting airway diameter and the smoothness of gas inflow and outflow [37,38].

Furthermore, imbalances in other neurotransmitters such as acetylcholine also occur during the pathogenesis of PD. In the lungs, acetylcholine participates in regulating physiological processes such as airway smooth muscle contraction and glandular secretion. When PD causes an imbalance of neurotrans mitters in the brain, it may further alter the release and action of acetylcholine in the lungs through mechanisms such as neural reflexes, increasing airway reactivity and predisposing to spasms, thereby exacerbating respiratory dysfunction [39,40]. Additionally, changes in neurotransmitters like serotonin (5Hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT) may also adversely affect pulmonary blood circulation and gas exchange through complex neural regulatory networks, collectively contributing to the process by which PD affects pulmonary function.

The mechanism of the Lung-Brain axis related signaling pathway in PD

Numerous signaling molecules are involved in the mechanisms linking the lung-brain axis to PD, with inflammation-related signaling molecules playing a crucial role. For instance, TNFa is released by immune cells in lung tissue when the lungs are stimulated by external factors such as infection or oxidative stress [41]. TNFa not only triggers local inflammatory responses in the lungs, causing lung tissue damage, but it can also be transported to the brain through blood circulation. In the brain, TNFa activates microglia, transitioning them from a resting (M1) state to an activated (M2) state, which leads to the release of more inflammatory mediators and reactive oxygen species (ROS), exacerbating neuroinflammatory responses and subsequently damaging dopaminergic neurons, thereby promoting the progression of PD [42,43]. Similarly, members of the IL family, such as IL-6, are upregulated during lung inflammation. They can regulate the neuroendocrine system, affecting the function of the hypothal amic-pituitary-adre nal (HPA) axis, altering hormone levels in the body, which in turn feedback to influence brain neuronal activity and neurotransmitter metabolism [44,45]. Additionally, elevated IL-6 can cross the BBB or participate in brain inflammatory responses indirectly by stimulating cerebrovascular endothelial cells, disrupting the integrity of the BBB, and allowing more peripheral harmful substances to enter the brain [46], thereby exacerbating the pathological changes in the brain of PD patients.

Lung inflammation in PD is initiated by resident immune cells, including alveolar macrophages and pulmonary dendritic cells, which sense pathogens or oxidative stress (e.g., from air pollution or cigarette smoke) and secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNFa and IL-6 [47]. Unlike systemic inflammation, the lungbrain axis features organ-specific immune crosstalk: pulmonary neuroendocrine cells (PNECs), unique to the respiratory epithelium, have been found to harbor a -synuclein aggregates, which may propagate retrogradely to the medullary dorsal motor nucleus via the vagus nerve, distinct from gut-derived pathways [48,49]. Alveolar macrophages, upon activation, release TNFa that specifically targets cerebral endothelial cells, upregulating adhesion molecules (e.g., intercellular cell adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1)) to promote leukocyte infiltration into the CNS [50]. This process is facilitated by the lung's proximity to the systemic circulation, allowing rapid transmission of inflammatory signals to the brain. Importantly, PD-related lung dysfunction is not merely a down- stream effect but can initiate neuroinflammation [51]. These fi ndings highlight the lung as a specific initiator of inflammation, with immune cell-mediated pathways distinct from other organs.

Furthermore, signaling molecules related to oxidative stress are also crucial in this process. The lungs come into contact with a large number of oxygen molecules during normal gas exchange, making them prone to generating ROS. When the body's antioxidant defense system is imbalanced, ROS accumulate significantly, leading to a state of oxidative stress [52]. These ROS directly damage lipids, proteins, and DNA in lung tissue cells, affecting lung function. Additionally, excessive ROS can reach the brain through blood circulation, interfering with the normal function of mitochondria in brain cells, causing mitochondrial energy production disorders, releasing pro-apoptotic factors such as cytochrome C (Cytc), and inducing neuronal apoptosis [53,54]. This is closely related to the pathological feature of progressive neuronal loss in PD (Fig. 2).

The Connection between Liver Metabolism and PD

The liver, as a vital metabolic organ in the human body, participates in the synthesis, decomposition, and conversion of numerous substances. Its metabolic functions have a non-negligible correlation with the occurrence and progression of PD. For instance, the cytochrome P450 enzyme system plays a crucial role in detoxification and metabolism in the liver. Mutations in some cytochrome P450 genes (such as the cytochrome P450 2D6 gene) may reduce the corresponding enzyme activity, impairing liver detoxification function. This makes the body more susceptible to toxins like 1-methyl-4-phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine (MPTP), thereby damaging the substantia nigra striatum and increasing the risk of PD [55,56]. Additionally, lipid metabolism abnormalities are often observed in PD patients. The liver is a significant site for lipid metabolism. When disruptions occur in the liver's synthesis, transport, and catabolism of lipids, it may affect the structure and function of neuronal membranes. Studies have found evidence of lipid peroxidation in the brains of PD patients [57], which may be indirectly related to abnormal liver lipid metabolism. Abnormal lipid metabolites may participate in the neurodegenerative process of PD, affecting the normal physiological functions of neurons.

From the perspective of nutrient metabolism, the liver is involved in the metabolism and storage of various vitamins and trace elements. Abnormalities in the metabolism of vitamins such as vitamin D and minerals like iron are also commonly found in PD patients [58,59]. Impairments in the hydroxylation and activation of vitamin D in the liver may hinder vitamin D's protective role in the nervous system. Moreover, disturbances in iron metabolism lead to increased iron ion concentrations and decreased ferritin levels in the brain, making the substantia nigra vulnerable to oxidative stress. This further exacerbates the pathological changes of PD, as oxidative stress plays a pivotal role in the pathogenesis of PD. Excessive free radical production can disrupt cell membranes, damage proteins and DNA, and promote neuronal degeneration and death.

The relationship between liver detoxification function and PD

The liver's detoxification function is crucial for maintaining the stability of the body's internal environment and is closely linked to the pathogenesis of PD [60,61]. On the one hand, exogenous toxic substances, such as certain environmental toxins like pesticides and herbicides, enter the human body and undergo metabolic detoxification in the liver. ## Impact of PD on the liver

## Burden of medication Therapy on the liver

The treatment of PD often necessitates prolonged use of medications such as levodopa, dopamine receptor agonists, and MAOB inhibitors, which undergo hepatic metabolism. Long-term use of these drugs may lead to hepatotoxicity, as evidenced by elevated liver enzymes (e.g., alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST)) and, in rare cases, drug-induced liver injury (DILI) [66,67]. For instance, levodopa undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism in the liver, and its metabolites may contribute to oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction in hepatocytes [68]. Clinical research reports indicate that although severe DILI is rare, transient elevation of liver enzymes occurs in PD patients treated with levodopa [66]. Similarly, dopamine receptor agonists (e.g., ropinirole, pramipexole) and MAO-B inhibitors (e.g., rasagiline) are metabolized via cytochrome P450 enzymes, with potential hepatotoxic effects reported in clinical trials

[69,70]. As a COMT inhibitor, tolcapone has significant hepatotoxicity, which may lead to a marked increase in patients' ALT/AST levels. Therefore, strict monitoring is required [71].

Current clinical guidelines emphasize the critical role of liver function monitoring and personalized dose adjustment for PD patients taking hepatotoxic medications. The International Parkinson and Movement Disorder Society (MDS) advises baseline liver function tests (LFTs) prior to initiating levodopa or dopamine agonists, followed by periodic monitoring, especially for those with pre-existing liver issues or on multiple drugs [72,73]. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) mandates regular LFTs for medications like tolcapone, requiring discontinuation if ALT/AST levels exceed three times the upper limit of normal (ULN) [71,74]. For dose adjustment, mild hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh A) warrants a reduction in levodopa dosage to prevent drug accumulation, while severe impairment (Child-Pugh B/C) calls for avoiding non-essential hepatotoxic agents [75]. In cases of elevated liver enzymes, specialists may recommend switching to nonhepatoto xic alternatives (e.g., transdermal rotigotine) or adjunct therapies (e.g., safinamide) [76,77]. The European Medicines Agency (EMA) stresses patient education on early signs of liver injury (e.g., jaundice, fatigue) and prompt discontinuation of suspect medications [78]. Emerging biomarkers like glutathione Stransferase a (GST a ) are being explored to detect subclinical hepatotoxicity early in PD populations [79]. These strategies balance therapeutic efficacy with risk mitigation, underscoring the need for individualized approaches in PD management to address liver-related safety concerns.

Mechanisms of Liver-Brain axis related signaling pathways in PD

During the involvement of the liver-brain axis in the pathogenesis of PD, multiple key signaling pathways play crucial roles. Among them, the JAK/STAT signaling pathway has garnered significant attention. In the liv er, when stimulated by inflammatory responses, meta- bolic stress, or other conditions, signal molecules such as cytokines can activate JAK proteins, which then phosphorylate and activate STAT proteins. The activated STAT proteins translocate to the nucleus to regulate the expression of a series of genes [86]. These genes encode numerous metabolism-related enzymes, transporters, and other proteins, influencing the liver's metabolic functions and altering the production and release of metabolites. When these metabolites enter the bloodstream and reach the brain, they can affect the activity of the JAK/STAT signaling pathway within brain neurons. For example, they may alter the expression of genes related to neurotransmitter synthesis in neurons, leading to neurotransmitter imbalances. In PD, abnormal ac tivation of the JAK/STAT signaling pathway within dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra region of the brain promotes inflammatory responses, apoptosis, and other pathological changes [87,88], which are closely related to the n eurodegenerative process of PD.

The Wnt/ b -catenin signaling pathway also plays a significant role in the connection between the liver-brain axis and PD. In normal liver cells, Wnt proteins bind to receptors on the cell membrane, inhibiting the degradation of b -catenin, allowing it to accumulate in the cytoplasm and enter the nucleus to regulate cell proliferation, differentiation, and the expression of metabolismrelated genes, thereby maintaining normal liver physiological functions. However, when the liver is damaged or in a pathological state, this signaling pathway may be inhibited or abnormally activated, leading to liver metabolic disorders. This results in a reduction in substances that normally pro vide neuroprotective effects on neurons and an increase in harmful metabolites that are transported to the brain [89,90]. In the brains of PD patients, Wnt/ b -catenin signaling pathway abnormalities are also associated with abnormal proliferation and differentiation of neural stem cells and changes in neuronal synaptic plasticity, further affec ting the disease progression of PD [91], such as exacerbating motor dysfunction in patients.

The mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) signaling pathway plays a pivotal role in the interplay between hepatic dysfunction and PD progression. In the liver, mTOR integrates nutrient and stress signals to regulate autophagy, lipid metaboli sm, and detoxification. Dysregulation of hepatic mTOR, often triggered by oxidative stress or metabolic overload, impairs autophagy-mediated clearance of damaged organelles and misfolded proteins (e.g., a -synuclein) [92,93] (Fig. 3). These dysfunctional hepatocytes release pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6) and lipid peroxidation products into systemic circulation, which traverse the BBB and exacerbate neuroinflammation [94]. Concurrently, mTOR hyperactivation in dopaminergic neurons disrupts lysosomal degradation of a -synuclein aggregates, accelerating neuronal apoptosis [95]. Notably, hepatic and neuronal mTOR dysregulation occurs in parallel but is mechanistically interconnected: hepatic-derived metabolites (e.g., ceramides) directly inhibit neuronal mTOR, while neuroinflammation reciprocally suppresses hepatic autophagy via TNFa /NFj B signaling [96,97]. Preclinical studies demonstrate that liver-specific mTOR inhibition (e.g., rapamycin) reduces a -synuclein burden in PD model by enhancing hepatic detoxification and restoring neuronal autophagy [98]. Similarly, hepatic overexpression of mTOR suppressor TSC1 mitigates dopaminergi c neurodegeneration, confirming bidirectional crosstalk [99]. Clinical trials (NCT04629495) are evaluating rapamyci n's efficacy in PD patients [100], with preliminary data showing reduced serum neuroinflammatory markers and improved motor scores.

## Heart-Brain axis and PD

The role of the heart in PD

In recent years, the significance of the heart-brain axis in the pathogenesis of PD has increasingly garnered attention. Epidemiological studies have found that the risk of cardiovascular diseases is elevated in PD patients compared to the general population, with PD patients more prone to conditions such as arrhythmia and abnormal blood pressure fluctuations [101]. Furthermore, the presence of cardiovascular diseases may, in turn, affect the progression rate and severity of PD symptoms [102], suggesting a bidirectional relationship between the two conditions.

From a pathophysiological perspective, the heart is not merely a pump for blood circulation; it also possesses neuroendocrine functions. The heart can secrete various bioactive substances, including atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) and brain natriuretic peptide (BNP), which, through blood circulation, act on organs and tissues throughout the body, including the brain. In PD, studies have found abnormal changes in the levels of these neuroendocrine factors in patients, suggesting that alterations in cardiac secretory function may be involved in the pathogenesis of PD [103,104].

Simultaneously, the heart's pumping function is crucial for maintaining normal blood perfusion in the brain. The dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra region of the brain in PD patients are sensitive to ischemia and hypoxia. Any abnormality in heart function leading to insufficient blood supply to the brain may accelerate the degeneration and death of these neurons, thereby influencing the progression of PD. Additionally, imaging studies and other diagnostic methods in PD p atients have revealed subtle changes in cardiac structure and function in some patients, further supporting the close link between the heart-brain axis and PD.

The impact of PD on the cardiovascular system

## Effects on cardiac structure and function

PD profoundly impacts cardiac structure and function through both neurodegenerative and iatrogenic mechanisms. Neuropatho- logically, PD reduces cardiac innervation due to degeneration of autonomic neurons in the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus and sympathetic ganglia, leading to impaired regulation of heart rate and contractility. This manifests clinically as arrhythmias (e.g., bradycardia, tachycardia) and reduced ejection fraction, which correlate with disease severity [105]. Long-term use of PD medications exacerbates cardiac dysfunction. Levodopa increases oxidative stress in cardiomyocytes by generating reactive metabolites (e.g., 3-O-methyldopa), which impair mitochondrial respiration and promote fibrosis [106]. Dopamine agonists (e.g., ropinirole, pramipexole) overstimulate cardiac D1/D2 receptors, disrupting calcium homeostasis and increasing arrhythmia risk [107]. Notably, ergot-derived agonists (e.g., pergolide) are linked to valvular fibrosis via serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine; 5-HT) receptor activation, a mechanism validated by echocardiographic studies showing thickened valve leaflets in PD patients [108]. monoamine oxidase-B (MAO-B) inhibitors (e.g., rasagiline) reduce catecholamine clearance, potentiating sympathetic overactivity and hypertension [109]. These drug-induced changes create a vicious cycle: cardiac output decline exacerbates cerebral hypoperfusion, accelerating dopaminergic neuron loss in the substantia nigra.

## Impact on vascular elasticity and blood pressure regulation

The autonomic nervous dysfunction caused by PD extends to the vascular system, affecting vascular elasticity and blood pressure regulation mechanisms. Normally, vascular endothelial cells secrete various vasoactive substances to maintain normal vasoconstriction and dilation functions as well as elasticity. However, factors such as neurotransmitter imbalance and chronic inflammatory states in PD patients may damage vascular endothelial cells, leading to endothelial dysfunction and subsequent decreases in vascular elasticity and compliance [110].

In terms of blood pressure regulation, patients with PD are susceptible to abnormal blood pressure manifestations such as orthostatic hypotension , supine hypertension, and postprandial hypotension, due to disordered autonomic regulation of blood pressure [111,112]. When transitioning from a lying position to an upright position, the mechanisms for maintaining normal blood pressure through vasoconstriction and tachycardia cannot be activated in a timely manner, resulting in transient cerebral hypoperfusion. Similarly, after eating, the body fails to effectively accommodate the increased blood demand in the gastrointestinal tract by rationally redistributing blood throughout the body to ensure stable perfusion of vital organs such as the brain. Longterm fluctuations in blood pressure not only affect heart load and function but also expose cerebral vessels to an unstable hemodynamic environment, which can easily lead to cerebrovascular diseases and exacerbate cognitive impairments in PD patients.

However, in PD patients, this neural reflex regulatory mechanism may be disrupted. On the one hand, the brain's ability to regulate cardiac function decreases; on the other hand, abnormal signals fed back from the heart to the brain may also interfere with the brain's normal neural activities, creating a vicious cycle and exacerbating the condition of PD patients [115].

Cardiac inflammation in PD is primarily driven by myocardial resident macrophages and endothelial cells, which respond to autonomic dysfunction or ischemic stress by releasing TNFa and IL-6 [116]. Unlike the lung, the heart's role in inflammation is closely tied to vascular integrity: activated cardiac endothelial cells secrete vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), disrupting the BBB and promoting cytokine entry into the brain [117]. Clinical studies show that PD patients with cardiac autonomic neuropathy exhibit higher levels of cardiac troponin I, a marker of myocardial microdamage, which correlates with neuroinflammation severity [118]. The heart-brain axis is further distinguished by neuroendocrine-immune crosstalk: ANP secreted by cardiomyocytes, modulates microglial polarization in the brain, shifting them toward an anti-inflammatory M2 phenotype [119]. This cardiac-specific signaling pathway provides a mechanistic link between myocardial immune activation and neuroprotective responses, underscoring the heart's unique role in PD pathogenesis.

The role of signaling molecules in the Heart-Brain axis and PD

Numerous signaling molecules are involved in the mechanisms linking the cardio-cerebral axis to PD (Fig. 4). Among them, neurotrophic factors play a significant role [120]. For instance, BDNF, which can normally be secreted by cardiac cells in certain quantities, travels through the bloodstream to the brain, providing nutritional support and protection to neurons, and promoting neuronal growth, differentiation, and the maintenance of synaptic plasticity. In PD, due to changes in cardiac function or other factors, the secretion of BDNF may decrease, resulting in weakened protective effects on dopaminergic neurons and other neurons in the brain, making them more susceptible to damage and accelerating the pathological process of PD. Nitric oxide (NO) is another crucial signaling molecule with extensive roles in both the cardiovascular and nervous systems [121]. In the heart, NO regulates the relaxation of vascular smooth muscle, maintaining normal blood pressure and blood flow. In the brain, NO participates in the regulation of neurotransmitter release and synaptic plasticity. Studies have found that the production and metabolism of NO may be abnormal in PD patients, affecting both the blood supply function of the heart and the signal transmission between neurons in the brain, thereby exacerbating both motor and non-motor symptoms in PD patients.

Cardiac macrophages secrete TNFa during PD-related myocardial stress, which activates cerebral endothelial NFj B [122,123]. This upregulates adhesion molecules (e.g., ICAM-1), enabling leukocyte migration into the brain and amplifying neu roinflammation [50]. Preclinical studies show TNFa inhibitors (e.g., etanercept) reduce dopaminergic neuron loss in MPTP mice [124]. ANP and BNP are elevated in PD due to cardiac stretch and hypoxia. While ANP enhances microglial anti-inflammatory M2 polarization via natriuretic peptide receptor (NPR), chronic overexpression downregulates dopamine transporter (DAT) expression in t he striatum, worsening motor symptoms [125]. ## The mechanism and signaling pathways of the Muscle-Brain axis in PD

The relationship between the Muscle-Brain axis and PD

There exists a multifaceted and intricate connection between muscles and the brain, forming a crucial foundation for the development and progression of PD within the context of the musclebrain axis. From a innervation perspective, motor neurons play a pivotal role in this connection. Their cell bodies are located in the anterior horn of the spinal cord or in motor nuclei of the brainstem, while their axons extend to innervate corresponding muscle fibers, forming the special structure of neuromuscular junctions. The brain transmits neural impulses to motor neurons through descending tracts such as the corticospinal tract, thereby regulating muscle contraction and relaxation to achieve various fine or gross motor movements [131,132]. In PD, degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra disrupts the transmission of neural impulses and regulation of muscles, leading to impairments such as decreased muscle coordination [133], highlighting the significance of neural disruption within the musclebrain axis in the pathogenesis of the disease.

Metabolically, muscles are one of the largest metabolic organs in the human body, participating in numerous metabolic processes such as glucose and lipid metabolism, and secreting various myokines, including irisin. These myokines can reach the brain through blood circulation, influencing the metabolic environment, energy supply, and neuroplasticity of neurons within the brain [134]. Conversely, the brain can also regulate muscle metabolism through neuroendocrine and other mechanisms. For instance, during stress, certain hormones secreted by the brain alter muscle metabolic rates and affect muscle function [135]. PD patients often exhibit alterations in muscle metabolism, with disorders in muscle energy metabolism and abnormal secretion of myokines potentially further influencing the function of brain neurons and participating in disease progression. Furthermore, muscles house important proprioceptive receptors. Receptors such as muscle spindles and tendon organs in muscles sense changes in muscle length and tension, transmitting these proprioceptive signals through sensory neurons to the spinal cord and subsequently to the brain. The brain uses this feedback information to adjust muscle contraction force and movement direction, maintaining normal body posture and motor balance [136]. Symptoms such as posture instability and balance disorders in PD patients are closely related to abnormalities in proprioceptive information transmission and processing within the muscle-brain axis.

The interaction between muscles and the brain extends beyond motor control, playing a critical role in both motor and non-motor symptoms of PD. While motor dysfunction (e.g., bradykinesia, rigidity) remains a hallmark of PD, emerging evidence highlights the muscle-brain axis's contribution to non-motor manifestations such as fatigue, depression, and cognitive decline [137]. For instance, skeletal muscle atrophy and metabolic dysfunction impair the secretion of neuroprotective myokines (e.g., irisin, BDNF), which normally cross the BBB to support dopaminergic neuron survival and synaptic plasticity. Deficits in these myokines correlate with depressive-like behaviors and cognitive impairment in PD models, suggesting a direct link between muscle-derived factors and non-motor symptoms [138,139]. Furthermore, proprioceptive feedback from muscle spindles and tendon organs is essential for postural stability and gait coordination. In PD, proprioceptive signaling disruptions contribute not only to balance impairments but also to anxiety related to movement unpredictability [140]. This underscores the muscle-brain axis's dual role in both motor and non-motor pathophysiology.

Mechanisms of Muscle-Brain axis related signaling pathways in PD

Impact of Muscle-Brain axis signaling pathways on motor and NonMotor symptoms

The mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling pathway regulates neuronal survival and muscle metabolism. In PD, oxidative stress and neuroinflammation dysregulate MAPK, impairing motor neuron function and reducing muscle contractility. Concurrently, MAPK abnormalities disrupt irisin secretion, exacerbating neuroinflammation and dopaminergic neuron loss [141,142]. In PD, due to pathological changes in the substantia nigra region of the brain, stimulated by factors such as oxidative stress and inflammatory responses, key proteins in the MAPK signaling pathway, such as extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK), may undergo abnormal activation or inhibition [143,144]. Skeletal muscle atrophy in PD disrupts the secretion of neuroprotective myokines (e.g., irisin, BDNF), which normally cross the BBB to support dopaminergic neuron survival via mTOR pathway [145]. The JAK/STAT pathway in skeletal muscle is activated by chronic inflammation, driving mitochondrial dysfunction and ROS overproduction [146]. Muscle-derived ROS propagate to the brain via circulation, amplifying oxidative damage in the substantia nigra. This bidirectional oxidative stress loop links muscle pathology to both motor decline and non-motor symptoms like fatigue and cognitive dysfunction [147,148]. Calcium signaling is pivotal for muscle contraction and neuronal activity. In PD, dysregulated calcium homeostasis in motor neurons disrupts neuromuscular junction stability, while intracellular calcium overload in dopaminergic neurons triggers apoptosis. These processes worsen motor symptoms and may contribute to neuropsychiatric manifestations via disrupted neurotransmitter balance [149,150].

## Bone-Brain axis and PD

The relationship between the Bone-Brain axis and PD

PD, as a complex neurodegenerative disorder, increasingly demonstrates a pathogenesis involving interactions among multiorgan axes, with the bone-brain axis serving as a crucial component. While the skeletal system primarily appears to support and facilitate body movement, it actually maintains close and intricate connections with the nervous system. These connections exert a non-negligible influence on the onset and progression of PD. Clinically, patie nts with PD often experience bone-related issues such as osteoporosis [162,163]. Studies on PD patients have detected abnormal levels of bone turnover markers, including type I collagen carboxyterminal peptide ( b -CTX) and Procollagen I NTerminal Propeptide (PINP). Specifically, patients in the osteoporosis group exhibited higher levels of PINP and b -CTX compared to those in the non-osteoporosis group. Correlation analyses further revealed a positive association between the oc currence of osteoporosis and b -CTX, PINP levels in PD patients [164,165]. These fi nd-

ings suggest a correlation between alterations in bone metabolism and PD, implying that these two systems may mutually influence each other and collectively contribute to the overall disease process.

From a physiological perspective, the skeleton is not an independent static structure but rather a dynamic entity with active metabolic regulatory mechanisms and bidirectional information exchange with the CNS. On the one hand, osteoblasts, osteoclasts, and other cells within the bone can secrete various cytokines, hormones, and other active substances, which can reach the brain via blood circulation and subsequently affect the brain's microenvironment, such as regulating neuronal metabolism and influencing neuroplasticity [166]. On the other hand, the brain can regulate bone metabolism through neuroendocrine and other pathways. When CNS function is abnormal, this regulatory balance is disrupted, potentially leading to bone metabolism disorders and bone loss [167]. Furthermore, animal experiments provide evidence for the role of the bone-brain axis in PD. For instance, in PD animal models, not only were dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra region of the brain observed to undergo progressive degeneration, but corresponding changes were also noted in bone structure and metabolic indicators [166]. This further suggests that the mechanism of mutual influence between the bone-brain axis plays a significant role in the pathogenesis and progression of PD, highlighting the importance of exploring the associations between these two systems.

Mechanisms of Bone-Brain axis related signaling pathways in PD

## The role of Bone-Brain axis in PD

In PD, bone-brain axis-related signaling pathways play a pivotal role in neurodegenerative changes. Among them, bone-derived cytokines serve as crucial mediators in this process. For instance, osteocalcin (OCN), an osteoblast-secreted protein, not only plays a significant regulatory role in bone metabolism but also possesses the ability to regulate brain function. Moreover, OCN can cross the BBB and directly bind to neurons in the hippocampal CA3 region, thereby influencing related brain functions [168]. Animal studies have shown that OCN can prevent motor impairments and dopaminergic neuron loss in 6-OHDA-induced PD mice [169]. When the gut microbiota was eliminated using antibiotics, the beneficial effects of OCN on motor function and the protection against dopaminergic neuron damage in mice were no longer effective [169]. This indicates that the neuroprotective effects of OCN on PD mice are mediated by the gut microbiota, revealing a complex mechanism where the bone-brain axis and the gutbrain axis are interconnected and jointly influence the neuropathological changes in PD. A clinical study has shown that plasma levels of OCN are significantly elevated in PD patients, while cerebrospinal fl uid (CSF) levels of OCN exhibit a significant inverse decrease. This differential distribution of OCN levels between these bodily fluids suggests that combined measurement of OCN concentration gradients in plasma and CSF could serve as a potential biomarker with greater discriminative power than single-index testing for distinguishing PD patients from healthy individuals, providing a more specific basis for evaluation in the early precise diagnosis of the disease [170]. The study found that in PD patients, OCN levels were 5.7 ± 4.2 ng/mL in women versus 3.5 ± 2.1 ng/mL in men. Female PD patients had an average of 2.6 ng/mL higher OCN levels than female controls, while male PD patients had 0.43 ng/mL lower levels than male controls; the interaction term was marginally significant ( p = 0.06). OCN levels showed no correlation with Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS) scores, disease duration, age, or body mass index (BMI). However, dopamine agonist medication dose was positively correlated with OCN levels (B = 0.0028, p = 0.03) [171]. Although no significant dif- ference in OCN levels was observed between PD patients and controls overall, preliminary evidence suggests that sex modifies the relationship between OCN levels and disease status. Notably, given the presence of dopamine receptors on osteoblasts and osteoclasts, the positive correlation between dopamine agonist use and OCN levels is intriguing. Collectively, these findings highlight the need for further research into the interplay among OCN, sex differences, and PD treatment.

Furthermore, other cytokines secreted by bone cells, such as transforming growth factorb (TGFb ), have also been found to be associated with PD neurodegeneration [172,173]. Under normal physiological conditions, TGFb participates in regulating cell proliferation, differentiation, and extracellular matrix synthesis, playing a vital role in bone tissue. However, in PD, the pathological environment of brain neurons may affect its expression levels. Altered TGFb then feedbacks to bone through blood circulation, affecting the function of bone cells. Simultaneously, it also influences neuropathological processes related to neuroinflammation and neuronal apoptosis in the brain, forming a vicious cycle that accelerates the progression of PD.

Additionally, bone-brain axis signaling can affect neurodegenerative changes by influencing the neuronal trophic support mechanism [174,175]. Small molecules produced during normal bone metabolism originally provide trophic support for neurons through the BBB or other pathways, maintaining their normal function and survival state. However, in PD, bone metabolic disorders disrupt the production and transportation of these nutrients, leading to insufficient nutritional supply to neurons. This makes neurons more susceptible to adverse factors such as oxidative stress and inflammatory responses, ultimately resulting in neurodegenerative changes such as degeneration and apoptosis.

## The role of Bone-Related signaling pathway in PD

The RANKL/RANK/OPG signaling pathway serves as a critical regulatory axis in the bone-brain interplay. Clinical evidence reveals elevated serum RANKL levels in PD patients, showing positive correlations with cerebrospinal fluid neuroinflam matory markers like TNFa and IL-6 [176]. Mechanistically, RANKL activates the NFj B pathway in microglia, driving pro-inflammatory cytokine release and exacerbating neuroinflammation in key brain regions such as the substantia nigra and hippocampus [177]. Bonederived signaling molecules demonstrate distinct roles in PD pathophysiology. OPG, a TNF receptor superfamily member, maintains bone homeostasis through osteoclast regulation [178]. In PD, altered OPG expression not only disrupts bone metabolism (predisposing to osteoporosis) but also engages in systemic inflammatory networks through cytokine interactions [179]. Aberrant OPG expression in the PD brain may dysregulate microglial activity, shifting these immune cells from homeostatic maintenance to pathological states characterized by excessive inflammatory mediator release and oxidative stress [176,180], processes intimately linked to PD neurodegeneration. The RANKL-OPG axis exhibits particular significance due to their functional antagonism. PD pathology disrupts their physiological balance, with RANKL upregulation driving dual pathological effects: enhanced osteoclast-mediated bone resorption and NFj B pathway activatio n in brain neurons via systemic circulation [181,182]. This imbalance may exacerbate neuroinflammation, neurodegeneration, and cognitive dysfunction. Furthermore, bone-specific communication molecules like connexins potentially mediate bone-neuron crosstalk. PDassociated bone inflammation involves coordinated osteoclast/osteoblast activity through RANKL-OPG secretion, with the RANKL/ RANK pathway uniquely enabling bone-to-brain communication via TNFa /IL-6 targeting of hippocampal and nigral microglia. ## Gut-Brain axis and PD

The relationship between the Gut-Brain axis and PD

The gut-brain axis plays a pivotal role in PD pathogenesis through bidirectional communication mediated by neural, endocrine, and immune pathways. Clinically, PD patients frequently exhibit gastrointestinal dysfunction, such as constipation, which often precedes motor symptoms by years. Emerging evidence suggests that a -synuclein pathology may originate in the ENS and propagate retrogradely to the brain via the vagus nerve [187]. Postmortem studies reveal a -synuclein aggregates in the ENS of PD patients, and animal models demonstrate that intragastric administration of a -synuclein fibrils induces nigrostriatal degeneration, supporting the ''gut-first' hypothesis of PD pathogenesis

[188,189]. Gut microbiota dysbiosis is a key contributor to this process. PD patients exhibit reduced microbial diversity, with decreased Prevotellaceae and increased Enterobacteriaceae, which correlate with intestinal inflammation and elevated systemic proinflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNFa , IL-6) [10]. Dysbiosis disrupts intestinal barrier integrity (''leaky gut'), allowing bacterial endotoxins like LPS to enter systemic circulation. LPS activates Tolllike receptor 4 (TLR4) on microglia, triggering NFj B-mediated neuroinflammation and exacerbating dopaminergic neuron loss [190].

Recent studies highlight specific microbial metabolites that modulate a -synuclein pathology. Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), such as butyrate, produced by Clostridia species, enhance intestinal barrier function by upregulating tight junction proteins (e.g., occludin, zonula occludens-1 (ZO-1)) via GPR41/43 signaling [191]. Butyrate also inhibits histone deacetylases (HDACs), promoting anti-inflammatory Treg cell differentiation and reducing neuroinflammation [192]. Conversely, dysbiosis-driven overproduction of trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) exacerbates a -synuclein aggregation by inducing oxidative stress and impairing autophagy-lysosomal pathways [193]. Additionally, gut-derived curli amyloid proteins from Escherichia coli cross-seed a -synuclein fi brillization through molecular mimicry. This process is facilitated by bacterial extracellular vesicles (BEVs), which transport amyloidogenic proteins across the gut epithelium, activating enteric glial cells and promoting a -synuclein misfolding [194].

The gut-brain axis exerts a non-negligible influence on the onset and progression of non-motor symptoms in PD. In terms of constipation, a prevalent non-motor symptom, abnormalities in neurotransmission and endocrine regulation between the brain and the intestine s due to the gut-brain axis can weaken intestinal motility. This leads to prolonged fecal residence time in the intestines, excessive water absorption, and subsequently, dry and hard stools, ultimately causing constipation [202]. Concurrently, constipation can adversely affect the intestinal environment, further exacerbating intestinal dysbiosis. The inflammatory responses triggered by dysbiosis can be transmitted to the brain through signaling pathways, impacting brain neural function and creating a vicious cycle that aggravates the overall condition of PD. PDassociated dysbiosis reduces 5-HT precursor availability, leading to depressive-like behaviors [204]. Restoring 5-HT through Bacteroides fragilis BF839 supplementation improved anxiety and cognition in PD mice [205].

## Clinical trials on Microbiota-Mo dulating therapies

Clinical trials targeting the gut microbiota have shown promise. Probiotics, such as Lactobacillus plantarum PS128 (NCT04884165), improved motor symptoms and reduced inflammatory markers (IL-6, TNFa ) in PD patients [12]. Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT), as demonstrated in a pilot study (NCT03808389), showed that FMT from healthy donors alleviated constipation and nonmotor symptoms (depression, sleep disorders) in PD patients [211]. Dietary interventions with high-fiber diets enriched with polyphenols reduced a -synuclein oligomerization in PD models [212]. Additionally, studies on oat phenolic compounds reveal that those with stronger antioxidant capacity can promote the proliferation of beneficial gut microbiota (such as Lactobacillus/Enterococcus and Bifidobacterium) and inhibit the growth of harmful microbiota (such as Bacteroides and Clostridium/histolyticum) [213]. In summary, there is a positive correlation between the antioxidant activity and prebiotic effect of oat phenolic com- pounds. These fi ndings underscore the therapeutic potential of dietary interventions targeting microbial-metabolic networks.

## The integrated role of multi-organ axis in PD

Interplay between multi-organ axis and PD

The onset and progression of PD are not caused by lesions in a single organ; rather, they result from complex and intimate interactions among multi-organ axes, collectively influencing the disease course. The lung-brain axis, liver-brain axis, heart-brain axis, muscle-brain axis, bone-brain axis, and gut-brain axis each engage in information communication and mat erial exchange with the brain through distinct mechanisms (Fig. 8). Meanwhile, these axes also interact and regulate each other. Dysbiosis of the gut microbiota (such as excessive proliferation of pathogenic bacteria) induces intestinal mucosal barrier damage, promoting the entry of inflammatory cytokines such as TNFa and IL-6 into the bloodstream and forming a systemic inflammatory response [195]. These cytokines activate the JAK/STAT signaling pathway in t he liver [87,88], inhibiting the activity of hepatic detoxifying enzymes (e.g., cytochrome P450), exacerbating the accumulation of neurotoxins (e.g., MPTP), and disrupting lipid metabolism-thereby causing structural damage to the membranes of d opaminergic neurons in the brain [55,56]. TNFa impairs vascular endothelial cells, reducing NO production, which leads to decreased vascular elasticity and autonomic nervous system dysfunction, inducing orthostatic hypotension commonly seen in PD patients. Concurrently, IL-6 activates cardiac fi broblasts, promoting myocardial fi brosis and affecting cardiac output and cerebral blood perfusion. Common pathways such as neuroinflammation (e.g., NFj B pathway), oxidative stress (e.g., ROS accumulation), and mitochondrial dysfunction link the pathological processes of multiple organ axes. For example, ROS generated by abnormal mitochondrial function in skeletal muscle can enter the circulation, exacerbating oxidative

damage in the lungs and intestinal mucosal barrier disruption, forming a vicious cycle among the muscle-lung-intestine axes [214,215]. Iron overload caused by hepatic metabolic disorders induces osteoclast activation in bone tissue (via the RANKL pathway) through blood circulation [181,182], while also promoting microglial activation in the brain and worsening neuroinflammation.

The pathogenesis of PD is driven by a dynamic, time-dependent interplay among multi-organ axes, forming a complex network that evolves with disease progression. In the early stage, gutbrain axis dysfunction initiates systemic inflammation via dysbiosis and a -synuclein propagation, triggering neuroinflammation and dopaminergic neuron loss [188,189]. Concurrently, hepatic detoxification pathways (e.g., cytochrome P450 enzymes ) become impaired, exacerbating neurotoxin accumulation [216-219]. By the mid-term phase, lung-brain axis involvement emerges, characterized by oxidative stress and hypoxemia, which further amplifies mitochondrial dysfunction in neurons. Cardiac autonomic dysfunction reduces cerebral blood flow, creating a hypoxic microenvironment that accelerates neurodegeneration [220-222]. In the late stage, muscle atrophy and bone metabolic disruptions (e.g., reduced OCN) impair neuroprotective signaling, while systemic inflammation from peripheral organs perpetuates a vicious cycle of neuronal damage [223-225]. This temporal progression highlights bidirectional feedback loops. In the positive feedback loop, gut-derived cytokines (e.g., TNFa ) enhance hepatic inflammation, which in turn increases BBB permeability, allowing neurotoxins to infiltrate the brain. For the negative feedback loop, bone-derived OCN initially mitigates neuroinflammation but declines as osteoporosis progresses, exacerbating cognitive decline.

## The impact of Multi-Organ axis Synergy on PD

The synergistic action of multi-organ axes plays a pivotal role in the progression of PD, where they interact and influence each other, driving the disease forward. In the early stages of PD, intestinal dysbiosis and dysfunction of the enteric nervous system may first emerge, triggering chronic intestinal inflammation through the gut-brain axis. This leads to increased BBB permeability, immune cell activation and infiltration, prompting neuroinflammatory responses in the CNS and damaging neurons. The abnormal changes in the gut-brain axis at this stage set the stage for the onset of the disease and begin to affect other organ axes [226,227]. As the disease progresses, the liver, influenced by abnormalities in the gut-brain axis or experiencing metabolic alterations due to other internal and external factors, affects the balance of neurotransmitters and the metabolic environment of neurons in the brain through the liver-brain axis [228]. This further exacerbates neuronal damage, leading to the gradual emergence of motor and non-motor symptoms, such as bradykinesia, resting tremor, sleep disorders, and emotional disturbances. Meanwhile, metabolic disturbances in the liver feedback to the intestine, exacerbating intestinal dysbiosis and perpetuating and worsening abnormalities in the gut-brain axis.

Regarding the lung-brain axis, as PD progresses, patients are prone to respiratory dysfunction and increased susceptibility to infections in the lungs. Enhanced inflammatory responses and elevated levels of oxidative stress in the lungs exert effects on the brain through neural conduction, blood circulation, and endocrine regulation. These pathological changes in the lungs not only directly damage dopaminergic neurons in the brain but also interact synergistically with adverse effects transmitted from other organ axes, accelerating neuronal degeneration and death, and promoting the neurodegenerative process of PD [229]. Additionally, cardiac function is also affected during the progression of PD. Abnormaliti es in the heart-brain axis lead to alterations in car- diac pumping function and dysregulation of neuroendocrine factor secretion, resulting in insufficient blood supply to the brain and neuroregulatory disturbances. This not only impacts the brain's own functions but also affects blood perfusion and functional regulation in other organs [230], such as disrupting liver metabolism by affecting its blood supply and impairing muscle energy metabolism and normal contractile and diastolic functions. The interactions among various organ axes continuously exacerbate both motor and non-motor symptoms in PD patients, leading to a sustained decline in their quality of life.

Changes in muscles and bones cannot be overlooked as well. In the muscle-brain axis, muscle atrophy, weakness, and poor coordination, along with bone metabolism abnormalities and osteoporosis in the bone-brain axis, not only affect patients' motor abilities and physical condition but also interact with other organ axes through various mechanisms [231]. For instance, muscle atrophy reduces physical activity, further decreasing cardiopulmonary function and affecting the cardio-encephalic and lung-brain axes [232,233]. Factors released due to abnormal bone metabolism affect blood circulation and neuroregulation, adversely impacting organs such as the brain, heart, and liver. These multi-organ axes work together to evolve the condition of PD, progressing from relatively mild symptoms in the early stages to more severe motor and non-motor dysfunction in the later stages, severely impacting patients' daily lives, physical and mental health.

Role of integrated signaling pathways in the multi-organ axis in PD

During the interaction among the multi-organ axes in PD, multiple integrated signaling pathways play pivotal roles, tightly linking various organ axes and coordinating information transfer and functional regulation among organs. For instance, inflammationrelated signaling pathways are ubiquitous and interconnected across multi-organ axes. In the gut-brain axis, gut microbiota dysregulation triggers intestinal inflammation, prompting immune cells to release inflammatory factors such as TNFa and ILs. These inflammatory factors can be transported to the brain through blood circulation, activating microglial cells, initiating neuroinflammatory responses, damaging dopaminergic neurons, and promoting the progression of PD [234,235]. Similarly, in the lungbrain axis, when the lungs are stimulated by external factors such as infection or oxidative stress, immune cells within lung tissue also release inflammatory factors like TNFa , which not only cause inflammatory damage to the lungs themselves but also affect the brain through blood circulation, exacerbating neuroinflammation [236]. The mechanism of action is similar to that of inflammatory signaling in the gut-brain axis, and there is mutual influence between them. Intestinal inflammatory factors may affect the immune status of the lungs, while lung inflammation may feedback to influence gut microbiota and intestinal inflammatory responses, jointly impacting PD progression through inflammation-related signaling pathways.

Among these, the JAK/STAT signaling pathway acts as a central hub for inflammatory crosstalk. In the gut-brain axis, dysbiotic microbiota induce intestinal epithelial cells to secrete IL-6, which activates hepatic JAK/STAT to downregulate cytochrome P450 enzymes, impairing neurotoxin clearance and promoting a -synuclein aggregation in dopaminergic neurons [87,237]. Concurrently, lung-derived TNFa activates JAK/STAT in brain microglia, enhancing their pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype and exacerbating neuronal damage [238]. This cross-organ amplification of JAK/STAT signaling creates a systemic inflammatory milieu that accelerates PD progression. The Wnt/ b -catenin pathway coordinates neurotrophic and metabolic interactions across axes. In the bonebrain axis, osteoblast-secreted Wnt ligands promote hepatic b -catenin-dependent synthesis of neuroprotective metabolites (e.g.,

OCN), which cross the BBB to support hippocampal neuroplasticity [239,240]. Conversely, hepatic fi brosis disrupts Wnt/ b -catenin signaling, reducing OCN production and worsening cognitive decline in PD [239,240]. In skeletal muscle, irisin activates muscle Wnt/ b -catenin to enhance neuromuscular junction stability, a function impaired in PD due to muscle atrophy and Wnt ligand deficiency [241]. These examples illustrate how Wnt/ b -catenin acts as a metabolic-neural bridge, with its dysfunct ion in one organ propagating deficits across the multi-organ network.

Neuroendocrine-related signaling pathways also permeate multi-organ axes. The liver, through the liver-brain axis, influences the metabolism and balance of brain neurotransmitters. Some substances or metabolites secreted by the liver can regulate the neuroendocrine system, such as affecting the function of the HPA axis and altering hormone levels in the body. These hormonal changes, in turn, feedback to affect brain neuronal activity and neurotransmitter metabolism, while also influencing the functional states of other organs such as the heart and muscles [242,243]. Neuroendocrine factors secreted by the heart, such as ANP and BNP, not only participate in regulating physiological functions such as body fluid and salt balance and blood pressure but also affect neuronal activity in the brain [244,245]. Together with related substances secreted by organs such as the liver and intestine, they constitute a complex neuroendocrine regulatory network that acts on PD's neurological function and overall disease progression through corresponding signaling pathways.

Furthermore, oxidative stress-related signaling pathways and autophagy-related signaling pathways also play significant roles in the multi-organ axes. Each organ produces oxidative stress products such as ROS under normal physiological conditions and PD pathological states. When the body's antioxidant defense system is imbalanced, ROS accumulate extensively, forming an oxidative stress state that causes cellular damage and impairs function in organs such as the lungs, liver, heart, muscles, bones, and brain. These organs interact through blood circulation and other pathways, allowing oxidative stress-related damage to be continuously transmitted and amplified across the multi-organ axes, accelerating neurodegeneration in PD [246,247]. Autophagy-related signaling pathways play crucial roles in maintaining intracellular homeostasis and clearing abnormal proteins in organs such as the liver and brain. In PD, changes in hepatocyte autophagy levels affect the production and release of metabolites, influencing neuronal autophagy in the brain through the liver-brain axis [248,249]. Impaired neuronal autophagy in the brain further affects neuronal survival and function, jointly driving disease progression with abnormal changes in other organ axes. These integrated signaling pathways intertwine, forming a complex regulatory network for the multi-organ axes in PD.

## Clinical Relevance of Multi-Organ axes in PD

## Diagnostic and therapeut ic opportunities

The systemic nature of PD, mediated by multi-organ axes, provides novel opportunities for early diagnosis. For instance, dysbiosis and elevated inflammatory markers (e.g., TNFa , IL-6) in the gut correlate with early non-motor symptoms like constipation and depression. Fecal calprotectin and a -synuclein aggregates in colonic biop sies are being explored as diagnostic biomarkers, with studies showing sensitivity in pre-motor PD stages [250,251]. Reduced FEV and chronic hypoxia detected via spirometry may predict PD progression, as hypoxemia exacerbates neuronal oxidative stress [252]. Serum levels of cytochrome P450 enzymes (e.g., CYP2D6) and lipid peroxidation markers (e.g., malondialdehyde) could reflect impaired detoxific ation and metabolic dysfunction, aiding in risk stratification [253]. Gut microbiota-derived 5-HT deficiency correlates with mood disorders. Fecal microbiota transplan tation (FMT) in PD models restores 5-HT levels and alleviates anxiety [254]. Bone-derived OCN enhances hippocampal neuroplasticity. Low serum OCN levels in PD patients correlate with Mini-Mental Sta te Examination (MMSE) scores, suggesting therapeutic potential [255].

Bacteroides fragilis BF839 improves gut motility and reduces a -synuclein aggregation in PD mice [204]. Clinical trials (NCT04884165) are testing Lactobacillus plantarum PS128 for motor symptom alleviation [12]. Compound FLZ (TLR4/MyD88/ NFj B inhibitors) suppresses neuroinflammation via this pathway, showing efficacy in rotenone-induced PD models [199]. AFFITOPE ® PD01A, targeting gut-derived a -synuclein, is in Phase I trials to delay pathology propagation [130]. Ruxolitinib, a JAK1/2 inhibitor, reduces neuroinflammation and improves motor function in MPTP-treated mice [176]. Lithium chloride enhances Wnt signaling, promoting neurogenesis and protectin g dopaminergic neurons in PD models [256]. Rapamycin enhances autophagy, clearing a -synuclein aggregates in hepatocytes and neurons. A Phase II trial (NCT04629495) is assessing its safety in PD patients [100]. Inhaled corticosteroids (e.g., budesonide) reduce pulmonary TNFa , improving oxygenation and slowing neurodegeneration in PDCOPD overlap patients [257]. Nebulized glutathione mitigates oxidative stress in PD lungs, with a Phase I trial (NCT04394676) showing improved FEV1 and UPDRS scores [258]. BNP improves cerebral blood fl ow and reduces orthostatic hypotension in PD patients with cardiac dysfunction [259]. Pimavanserin, a serotonin 5-HT2A inverse agonist, avoids cardiac fi brosis risks associated with traditional dopamine agonists [260]. Recombinant irisin injections restore muscle-brain communication, enhancing motor coordination in PD mice [261]. Denosumab, an anti-RANKL monoclonal antibody, reduces bone resorption and neuroinflammation in PD-osteoporosis models [162].

## Challenges in Translating Multi-Organ axis therapies to Clinical Practice

Despite promising preclinical data, several barriers hinder clinical translation. Biological complexity is a significant issue, as targeting multiple axes risks off-organ toxicity; for example, systemic JAK/STAT inhibition may reduce neuroinflammation but impair hepatic regeneration. Individual variability across PD subtypes (such as tremor-dominant vs. postural instability) complicates standardized therapies, making personalized approaches guided by biomarker profiling essential. Drug development also faces hurdles: multi-target therapies encounter regulatory challenges, as exemplified by tolcapone's failure due to hepatotoxicity, which underscores the need for organ-specific delivery systems (e.g., nanoparticle-encapsulated agents). Trial design limitations are evident because current endpoints (e.g., UPDRS) inadequately capture multi-organ effects, necessitating composite metrics that integrate motor, cognitive, and systemic inflammatory markers. Ethical and regulatory hurdles require cross-disciplinary collaboration for therapies affecting multiple organ systems, thus delaying implementation. The AFFITOPE ® PD01A trial, targeting gut-derived a -synuclein, faced difficulties in demonstrating central efficacy due to variable gut-brain axis engagement across participants [262]. Similarly, inhaled glutathione improved lung function but showed limited BBB penetration, emphasizing the need for dual-target formulations [258].

## Conclusion

Impact of multi-organ axis Interconnecti vity on PD

The systemic nature of PD, mediated by interconnected multiorgan axes, underscores the necessity of moving beyond a purely brain-centric view. Our analysis reveals that the lung-brain, liver-

brain, heart-brain, muscle-brain, bone-brain, and gut-brain axes form a dynamic network where pathological changes in one organ cascade to others through shared pathways such as neuroinflammation, oxidative stress, and mitochondrial dysfunction. For instance, gut dysbiosis triggers a -synuclein aggregation, which propagates via the vagus nerve to the brain while simultaneously inducing systemic inflammation that exacerbates hepatic detoxification impairment and pulmonary oxidative stress. Similarly, cardiac autonomic dysfunction reduces cerebral blood flow, amplifying neuronal vulnerability to oxidative damage. These interactions create a vicious cycle that accelerates neurodegeneration. Importantly, bone-derived hormones like OCN and musclesecreted myokines (e.g., irisin) highlight bidirectional communication, where peripheral organs not only respond to but also actively modulate brain pathology. This integrative perspective challenges traditional models and emphasizes the need for holistic therapeutic strategies targeting cross-organ crosstalk.

## Limitations of t he study

While this review synthesizes emerging evidence on multiorgan axes in PD, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, many fi ndings derive from animal models, which may not fully recapitulate human PD pathophysiology, particularly non-motor symptoms. Second, clinical studies often lack longitudinal data, limiting insights into temporal relationships between organ dysfunction and disease progression. Third, the heterogeneity of PD subtypes (e.g., tremor-dominant vs. postural instability/gait difficulty) complicates the generalizability of multi-organ interactions. Additionally, confounding factors such as comorbidities (e.g., diabetes, cardiovascular diseases) and lifestyle variables (e.g., diet, exercise) were not systematically addressed but likely influence axis dynamics. Future studies should prioritize human cohort analyses with stratified subtyping and control for confounders to validate preclinical findings.

## Clinical Implications and Future directions

The multi-organ axis framework holds significant translational potential. Diagnostically, biomarkers from peripheral organs-such as fecal calprotectin (gut inflammation), serum OCN (bone metabolism), and spirometry metrics (lung function)-could enable early PD detection and subtyping. For example, colonic a -synuclein biopsies are being explored for pre-motor diagnosis (NCT04884165). Clinically, low serum OCN levels in PD patients correlate with cognitive decline, as evidenced by reduced MMSE scores, suggesting its utility as both a diagnostic marker and therapeutic target. Therapeutically, interventions targeting axis hubs may yield broader efficacy. Probiotics (e.g., Lactobacillus plantarum PS128) and fecal microbiota transplantation restore gutbrain communication, while JAK/STAT inhibitors (e.g., ruxolitinib) and mTOR modulators (e.g., rapamycin) mitigate neuroinflammation across organs. Repurposing denosumab (anti-RANKL) to concurrently address osteoporosis and neuroinflammation exemplifies multi-organ targeting. Clinically, inhaled antioxidants (e.g., nebulized glutathione) improve pulmonary and cerebral oxidative stress, whereas recombinant irisin injections enhance muscle-brain signaling in PD models. Ongoing trials (e.g., AFFITOPE ® PD01A targeting gut-derived a -synuclein) highlight the shift toward precision medicine. Future research should prioritize combinatorial therapies, leveraging organ-axis synergies to disrupt PD's systemic progression.

Future research should prioritize several key directions with specific methodological approaches to advance our understanding of PD and its systemic interactions. For the gut-brain axis, longitu- dinal cohort studies integrating metagenomic sequencing and metabolomics are needed to identify stage-specific dysbiosis patterns and microbial metabolites (e.g., SCFAs) influencing a -synuclein aggregation, complemented by causality testing using gnotobiotic mouse models. In exploring the liver-brain axis, CRISPR-Cas9-mediated knockout of cytochrome P450 enzymes in hepatocytes could clarify neurotoxin accumulation in PD models, while liver organoids co-cultured with midbrain dopaminergic neurons in microfluidic chips may elucidate bidirectional metabolic crosstalk. The lung-brain axis requires development of inhaled nanoparticle-based anti-inflammatory agents (e.g., TNFa inhibitors) to assess their dual effects on pulmonary oxidative stress and nigral neurodegeneration in PD-COPD overlap models. For the muscle-brain axis, single-nucleus RNA sequencing of PD patient-derived myocytes could map myokine (e.g., irisin) secretion pathways, with validation of neuroprotective effects via recombinant protein delivery in preclinical models. The bonebrain axis warrants investigation into OCN's role in hippocampal neurogenesis using conditional GPR158 knockout mice, alongside cognitive assessments in PD-osteoporosis cohorts. Integrated multi-organ models should leverage organ-on-a-chip platforms to simulate cross-axis interactions (e.g., gut-liver-brain) and screen combinatorial therapies targeting shared pathways like NFj B or mitochondrial dysfunction. Translational efforts must adhere to rigorous ethical standards. Animal studies should follow the 3Rs principles (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement), particularly when modeling systemic interactions. Clinical trials involving microbiota modulation (e.g., FMT) must ensure donor screening, informed consent, and address privacy concerns in multi-omics data sharing. Gene-editing approaches (e.g., CRISPR) require compliance with institutional biosafety guidelines and prioritize offtarget effect analyses. By integrating mechanistic specificity with ethical rigor, these strategies will deepen insights into PD's systemic pathology and accelerate the development of precision therapies targeting interconnected organ networks.
