## Introduction

Parkinson's disease (PD) is among the most common neurodegenerative disorders and is characterized primarily by motor impairments, imposing a great burden on society [1]. The main pathological features of PD are the degeneration, death, and loss of dopaminergic (DAergic) neurons in the nigrostriatal system [2]. PD is marked by a complex pathogenesis,	while	glial	activation	with	neuroinflammation	and oxidative stress damage attracted great attention [3-5].Additionally, emerging evidence suggests that astrocytes, traditionally	regarded	as	supportive	glial	cells,	actively	influence PD	progression	by	modulating	inflammatory	responses	and neuronal  survival  through  functional  heterogeneity  and dynamic interactions with various central nervous system (CNS)	cells	under	different	pathological	conditions	[ 6-8]. Recent	studies	emphasize	the	importance	of	the	effects	of the  gut  microbiota  and  metabolism  through  the  gut-brain axis	on	neuroinflammation	and	oxidative	stress	in	individu -als  with  PD  [9-11].  Therefore,  exploring  the  underlying mechanism	 of	 astrocyte-mediated	 neuroinflammation	 and neurodegeneration through alterations in the gut and brain during PD progression and in response to potential therapeutic interventions is important.

RRx-001 (RRx, 2-bromo-1-(3,3-dinitroazetidin-1-yl) ethanone)  is  a  novel  immunoregulatory  molecule  that directly  inhibits  NOD-like  receptor  family  pyrin  domain containing	3	(NLRP3)	inflammasome	activation	[ 12]. RRx can also downregulate MYC and CD47 expression in individuals with nonneurological diseases [13, 14]. In addition to	 its	 anti-inflammatory	 and	 antioxidant	 properties,	 RRx acts as a redox-active and epigenetic modulator that regulates	nitric	oxide	signalling,	inhibits	NLRP3	inflammasome activation,  and  supports  mitochondrial  homeostasis,  providing  a  multifactorial  basis  for  neuroprotection.  NLRP3 inflammasome	activation	has	been	verified	to	induce	neu -roinflammation	and	exacerbate	neuronal	damage	and	func -tional impairment in the CNS, all of which contribute to PD progression  [15].  In  the  CNS,  RRx  protects  against  LPSinduced	 neuroinflammation	 through	 the	 disruption	 of	 the TLR4	pathway	and	NLRP3	inflammasome	activation	[ 16]. However, few in vivo experimental studies  related  to  the effects	of	RRx	on	PD	have	been	published.	Due	to	the	lack of	understanding	of	the	mechanism	underlying	the	effect	of RRx on PD and the conclusions of these published studies, the function of RRx in protecting DAergic neurons remains to	be	confirmed.

Due	 to	 the	 importance	 of	 NLRP3	 inflammasome	 acti -vation  to  RRx,  we  then  consulted  researches  concerning NLRP3	inflammasome	to	explore	the	potential	mechanism whether	 RRx	 affects	 NLRP3	 inflammasome	 activation to  mediate  PD.  Composed of the innate immune receptor

NLRP3,  the  adaptor  protein  ASC,  and  the  protease  caspase-1,	NLRP3	inflammasome	activation	can	promote	the release	of	the	proinflammatory	cytokines	IL-1β	and	IL-18 and  induce  gasdermin  D-dependent  pyroptosis  [17]. As  a lipocalin	family	member,	lipocalin-2	(LCN2)	is	an	inflam -matory and immune protein [18, 19] and has been identified	 as	 an	 upstream	 regulator	 of	 NLRP3	 inflammasome activation  [20].Expressed  primarily  in  astrocytes  of  the CNS,	 increasing	 LCN2	 levels	 are	 linked	 to	 neuroinflam -mation, oxidative stress, stroke, and neurodegeneration in PD, ageing and other neurodegenerative diseases [21-23]. Moreover,  LCN2  has  recently  been  recognized  to  induce astrocyte senescence and accelerate the progression of PD [24 ]	 and	 astrocyte	 pyroptosis	 in	 response	 to	 neuroinflam -mation [20 ]	and	to	impede	α-synuclein	endocytosis	in	indi -viduals with PD [25].

Owing to the strong connection between astrocyte-associated	 neuroinflammation,	 LCN2-NLRP3	 inflammasome activation  and  pyroptosis,  which  are  all  associated  with PD, we hypothesize that RRx alleviates DAergic neurodegeneration and loss in individuals with PD by modulating astrocyte	 pyroptosis	 and	 attenuating	 neuroinflammation induced	by	LCN2-NLRP3	inflammasome	activation.	In	the present study, RRx was used as a strategy for PD therapy in an MPTP-induced PD mouse model. We demonstrated that RRx	ameliorated	motor	deficits	and	dopaminergic	neuron loss in MPTP-induced PD model mice. Further mechanistic  investigations  suggested  that  RRx  inhibited  astrocyte pyroptosis to protect DAergic neurons by targeting LCN2NLRP3	inflammasome	activation.	With	respect	to	metab -olism,  the  metabolomic  analysis  of  the  substantia  nigra revealed that RRx reduced the levels of metabolites associated  with  PD  and  downregulated PD and oxidative stress signalling pathways in the substantia nigra. In addition, 16 S	rDNA	sequencing	and	metabolomic	analyses	confirmed that  RRx altered the gut microbiota and fecal metabolites and	influenced	inflammatory	metabolism.	Given	that	neu -roinflammation	 and	 oxidative	 stress	 influence	 dopaminer -gic vulnerability, investigating whether RRx modulates PD phenotypes  via  astrocyte  pyroptosis  and  LCN2-NLRP3 inflammasome	activation	may	provide	novel	insights	into neuroinflammation-targeted	therapeutic	strategies	for	PD.

## Materials and methods

## Reagents and antibodies

MPTP (1-methyl-4-phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine) was	obtained	from	Sigma-Aldrich	(St.	Louis,	MO,	USA). RRx-001 (2-bromo-1-(3,3-dinitroazetidin-1-yl)ethanone) was  purchased  from  Selleck  Chemicals,  USA  (S8405).

Polyethylene  glycol  (PEG)  300  was  obtained  from  MCE (America),  and  Tween  80  (ST2789)  was  purchased  from Beyotime  Biotechnology  (China).  Antibodies  against  TH (sc-25269;  Santa  Cruz  Biotechnology,  USA),  GAPDH (60004-1;	Proteintech,	USA),	LCN2	(DF6816;	Affinity	Bio -science,  USA),  NLRP3  (19771-1-AP;  Proteintech,  USA), ASC  (sc-514414;  Santa  Cruz  Biotechnology,  USA),  caspase-1 (3866T; Cell Signaling Technology, USA), gasdermin	D	(93709T;	Cell	Signaling	Technology,	USA),	IL-1β (12703T; Cell Signaling Technology, USA), IL-18 (106631-AP;  Proteintech,  USA),  GFAP  (MAB360;  Millipore Sigma, USA), and Iba1 (ab289874; Abcam; UK) were used.

## Animals

Male C57BL/6J mice ( n = 40) aged 8 weeks (weight, 25 ± 2 g) were obtained from SPF Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). We used an online sample size calculator (  h  t  t p  s :  / /  c l  i n  c a  l c  . c  o m  /  s t  a t  s /  s a  m  p l  e  s  i  z  e  .  a  s  p  x) to calculate sample sizes. We raised these mice in a standard environmental temperature (20-24 °C) room with an adequate diet and regular lighting on a 12-h dark/light cycle (07:00, lights on; 19:00, lights	off).	Saline	and	MPTP	(25	mg/kg)	were	administered to the mice in several groups. Chronic PD model mice were constructed according to our previous protocol [26]. Randomly selected mice were assigned to the MPTP group and injected intraperitoneally with MPTP hydrochloride (25 mg/ kg) daily for 5 consecutive weeks, while the same number of mice in the control group was injected intraperitoneally with saline. Then, we dissolved RRx in DMSO to prepare a stock solution, which was subsequently diluted with PEG 300, Tween 80, and saline to the working concentrations. The	 effects	 of	 RRx	 on	 both	 PD	 model	 mice	 and	 control mice were explored after the compound was administered via intraperitoneal injection at doses of 5 mg/kg and 10 mg/ kg every other day for 5 consecutive weeks. We conducted behavioural tests within 7 days following treatment cessation  and  isolated  the  brain  tissue  for  further  experimental testing.  The  animal  care  and  experimental  procedures  of this  study  were  approved  by  the Animal  Ethics  Committee	of	The	First	Affiliated	Hospital	of	Guangzhou	Medical University.

## Behavior test

The	behavioural	tests	in	our	study	included	the	open	field test  (OFT),  pole-climbing  test,  rotarod  test,  and  grasping test, which were performed as described previously [27].

We proceeded with the OFT by placing the mice in the middle of the central area and observing them in dim light for  15  min;  their  behaviours  were  recorded  with  a  video tracking  system  and  EthoVision  XT  software  (Beijing,

China). We subsequently analysed the movement trajectories for total distance, speed, and central zone entries.

In the pole-climbing test, we used a vertical pole of 75 cm in height and 9 mm in diameter, which was covered with medical gauze for improved grip strength. After a 30-minute habituation  period,  each  mouse  was  positioned  at  the  top of the pole facing upwards and received training sessions for two consecutive days before the formal test. During the formal trial, a maximum duration of 60 s was set as the cutoff	point	for	observation.	The	time	(in	seconds)	required	for each mouse to climb from the top to the base of the pole was recorded.

The rotarod test  was  used  to  assess  the  motor  coordination  and  overall  balance  of  the  mice.  The  mice  were first	subjected	to	a	training	session	on	the	rotarod	appara -tus (Ugo Basile SRL, Gemonio, VA, Italy) operating at a constant speed of 10 rpm. Following a three-day interval, we placed  the  mice  on  the  rotarod  at  a  speed  increasing gradually from 4 rpm to 40 rpm within 5 min. The duration until each mouse fell from the apparatus, referred to as the latency to fall, was accurately recorded for the performance evaluation.

In  the  grasping  test,  the  mice  were  suspended  by  their forepaws on a 1-mm-diameter horizontal wire positioned 30 cm above the ground for 10 s. Grasping ability was scored as follows: 3 for using both hind paws, 2 for one hind paw, 1 for no hind paw use, and 0 if the mouse fell.

## Western blotting

After  the  substantia  nigra  tissue  was  collected,  we  used RIPA	lysis	buffer	supplemented	with	protease	and	phospha -tase inhibitors (Beyotime Biotechnology) to lyse the tissue and	quantified	the	protein	concentration	with	a	BCA	assay kit	 (Beyotime	 Biotechnology).	 After	 loading	 buffer	 was added,	the	proteins	were	denatured	at	100	°C.	SDS-PAGE was used to separate the sample proteins, which were subsequently transferred onto PVDF membranes. After blocking with 5% BSA, the membranes were incubated with primary antibodies,  followed  by  an  incubation  with  HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies overnight at 4 °C. Signals were detected  using  the  GeneGnome  XRQ  chemiluminescence system (Gene Company, Hong Kong), and band intensities were	quantified	using	ImageJ	software.

## Immunofluorescence staining

The mice were deeply anaesthetized and transcardially perfused	with	phosphate-buffered	saline	(PBS),	followed	by	4% paraformaldehyde (PFA). The brains were carefully dissected and	postfixed	with	4%	PFA	at	4	°C	for	12-24	h.	The	tissues were subsequently cryoprotected in graded sucrose solutions (10%,  20%,  and  30%  in  PBS)  until  they  fully  sunk. After being cryoprotected, the brains were embedded in OCT compound	and	rapidly	frozen	on	dry	ice.	Coronal	Sect.	(30	μm) were obtained using a freezing microtome (Leica) maintained at	-	 20	°C,	and	the	slices	were	blocked	and	incubated	with the corresponding primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C. The next	day,	the	brain	slices	were	incubated	with	a	fluorescent dye-labelled secondary antibody, and DAPI was used to stain the	nuclei.	The	images	were	scanned	under	a	confocal	laserscanning microscope (Zeiss LSM 900, Hamburg, Germany).

## RNA-sequencing

We removed the brain from each mouse and placed the brains on ice. The substantia nigra was dissected under a stereomicroscope  based  on  anatomical  landmarks  and  immediately snap frozen in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA was extracted using TRIzol reagent (Life Technologies, Carlsbad, CA, USA), and samples were treated with DNase to eliminate genomic DNA contamination. We subsequently used the NEBNext UltraTM RNA Library PrepKit for Illumina (NEB, USA) to construct sequencing	 libraries.	 Next,	 the	 PCR	 products	 were	 purified using the  AMPure XP system, and library quality was evaluated with an Agilent Bioanalyzer. Indexed libraries were clustered on a cBot system using the TruSeq PE Cluster Kit v4-cBotHS (Illumina)  according  to  standard  protocols.  Sequencing was performed on the Illumina platform to generate pairedend  reads.  Raw  data  were  processed  using  the  BMKCloud online pipeline (www.biocloud.net) and normalized using the DESeq2 median-of-ratios method to correct for library size and	 RNA	 composition	 bias	 prior	 to	 differential	 expression analysis [28], and p values were adjusted with the BenjaminiHochberg method to control the false discovery rate. All gene matrixes based on the counts of each sample were screened to	 identify	 differentially	 expressed	 genes	 (DEGs)	 using	 dif -ferential	 analysis	 software,	 with	 a	 fold	 change	 ≥	 1.5	 and	 a

Benjamini-Hochberg  adjusted  p-value  (FDR)  &lt;  0.05  as  the screening criteria. Functional annotation was conducted using Gene Ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analyses. The original RNA sequencing data have been submitted to the SRA, and the ID of the SRA data is PRJNA1348969. ## Metabolomic analysis

Metabolites were extracted using a 1:1 methanol-acetonitrile solution containing 2 mg/L internal standard. After ultrasonication in an ice bath for 10 min, the samples were centrifuged (12,000 rpm, 15 min, 4 °C). The supernatants were vacuumdried,  reconstituted  in  acetonitrile-water  (1:1),  vortexed, sonicated,	 and	 centrifuged	 again.	 The	 final	 extracts	 were transferred to vials, with 10 µL pooled for quality control.

The  analysis  was  performed  using  a  Waters  Acquity I-Class PLUS UPLC coupled with a Xevo G2-XS QTOF mass	 spectrometer	 and	 an	 HSS	 T3	 column	 (1.8	 μm, 2.1  ×  100  mm).  The  data  were  processed  by  Biomarker Technologies (Beijing, China), with metabolite annotations referencing  the  KEGG,  HMDB,  and  LIPID  MAPS  databases. Principal component analysis (PCA), principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) and Spearman's correlation analysis were	performed	to	assess	sample	consistency.	Differentially expressed	metabolites	(DEMs)	were	identified	using	t	tests, with  selection  thresholds  of p &lt;	 0.05	 and	 a	 VIP	 ≥	 1	 based on orthogonal projections to latent structures-discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA). The KEGG pathway enrichment analysis was performed via hypergeometric testing.

## Fecal 16 S rDNA analysis

Fresh fecal pellets were collected from individual mice and immediately	stored	at	-	80	°C.	Microbial	genomic	DNA	was extracted using a commercial stool DNA kit with mechanical disruption by bead beating. The DNA concentration and purity were assessed with a NanoDrop spectrophotometer. The V3-V4 region of the bacterial 16 S rDNA gene was amplified	by	PCR	using	barcoded	primers.	Amplicons	were purified	 with	AMPure	 XP	 beads,	 and	 library	 quality	 was confirmed	with	a	Qubit	fluorometer.	Sequencing	was	per -formed on the Illumina MiSeq platform using a paired-end 250 bp strategy. Raw reads were processed using QIIME2 for	quality	filtering,	denoising,	and	taxonomic	classification based  on  the  SILVA  database.  Projections  to  latent  structures-discriminant analysis (PLS-DA) demonstrated the difference among the three groups. Alpha and beta diversity metrics  were  calculated,  and  downstream  analyses  were conducted to assess the microbial composition.

## Statistical analysis

The  data  are  presented  as  the  means ± standard  errors  of the means (SEMs), and a p value &lt; 0.05 was considered to indicate	statistical	 significance,	 with	 significance	 denoted as * p &lt;  0.05 and ** p &lt;  0.01. Two-group comparisons were performed with Student's t test,  and  multigroup  analyses were performed with ANOVA (one-/two-way) and Tukey's post  hoc  test.  Metabolomic  data  were  evaluated  using

Hotelling's T² test. Statistical analyses were conducted in GraphPad Prism 10.0 (GraphPad Software, La Jolla, CA).

## Results

## RRx attenuated motor deficits and decreased DAergic neuronal loss in the MPTP-induced PD model

In	order	to	explore	the	effect	of	RRx	on	PD,	we	employed 8-week-old C57BL/6J mice to construct the MPTP-induced chronic PD model mice [33]. MPTP was injected intraperitoneally	into	the	C57BL/6J	mice	twice	a	week	for	five	con -secutive weeks, and the RRx intervention was administered three times a week. After the treatment was complete, behavioural  tests  were  conducted  (Fig.  1A).  Compared  with  the non-MPTP group, the MPTP group exhibited severe motor deficits	(Fig. 1B-G), whereas 5 and 10 mg/kg RRx reversed the MPTP-induced decreases in total distance travelled and mean	velocity	in	the	open	field	test	(Fig. 1B-D), holding time score  in  the  grasping  test  (Fig.  1E),  motor  capacity  in  the pole-climbing test (Fig. 1F), and latency to fall in the rotarod test (Fig. 1G).

Moreover,	we	confirmed	the	therapeutic	 effect	 of	 RRx on PD model mice by performing Western blotting to measure  the TH  protein  expression  levels  in  the  striatum  and substantia nigra. Western blot images and the quantitative analysis  showed  that  5  and  10  mg/kg  RRx  reversed  the MPTP-induced reduction in TH protein expression  levels in  both  regions  (Fig.  2 A-D). ## RRx reduced astrocyte pyroptosis to exert antineuroinflammation via inhibiting LCN2-NLRP3 inflammasome activation

As one of the key processes of the NOD-like receptor signalling	pathway,	the	NLRP3	inflammasome	activates	cas -pase-1	to	release	IL-18	and	IL-1β,	consequently	inducing pyroptosis [34 ].	NLRP3	inflammasome	activation	is	medi -ated  by  LCN2 to induce astrocyte pyroptosis, which promotes	neuroinflammatory	injury	[ 20].

Therefore, we hypothesized that RRx attenuates astrocyte  pyroptosis  by  downregulating  LCN2-NLRP3 inflammasome	 activation	 to	 decrease	 the	 loss	 of	 DAer -gic neurons and consequently reverse PD progression. We systematically	 evaluated	 the	 modulatory	 effects	 of	 RRx on	 NLRP3	 inflammasome	 activation	 in	 MPTP-induced PD model mice to elucidate the underlying mechanism. The  results  revealed  that  the  MPTP-induced  increases in	 the	 levels	 of	 NLRP3	 inflammasome	 activation	 and pyroptosis-associated  proteins  (NLRP3,  ASC,  pro-caspase-1, cleaved caspase-1, GSDMD-FL, GSDMD NTERM , mature	IL-1β,	and	IL-18)	were	reversed	by	the	adminis -tration of RRx (Fig. 5 A-B).	NLRP3	inflammasome	acti -vation	affects	the	downstream	protein	caspase-1	that	then cleaves GSDMD into GSDMD NTERM , leading to the crucial	 formation	 of	 pyroptosis	 pores	 to	 release	 inflamma -tory cellular contents [35]. We investigated whether RRx reduces  astrocyte  pyroptosis  through  the  inhibition  of NLRP3	inflammasome	activation	by	performing	immuno -fluorescence	staining.	As	shown	in	Fig. 5C-F, in the substantia nigra of PD model mice, the increased expression of the target proteins (GSDMD, NLRP3, and caspase-1) of	the	activated	NLRP3	inflammasome	was	significantly suppressed	by	RRx.	These	findings	collectively	demon -strated  that  RRx  alleviated  pyroptosis  in  astrocytes  of the	 substantia	 nigra	 to	 prevent	 neuroinflammation	 by suppressing	 LCN2-NLRP3	 inflammasome	 activation	 in MPTP-induced PD model animals.

## RRx improved gut microbiota dysbiosis and metabolism in the MPTP-induced PD model mice

The gut microbiota and metabolism play vital roles in PD progression  via  the  regulation  of  the  gut-brain  axis  [37]. Thus, we performed 16 S rDNA sequencing and a metabolomic analysis to explore the link between the gut condition and	PD.	PLS-DA	was	performed	to	illustrate	distinct	differ -ences  in  the  gut  microbiota  composition  among  the  three groups (Fig. 7A-B). The Shannon index was calculated to assess microbial diversity, and boxplots and line charts were generated	to	visualize	the	differences	in	microbial	diversity measured using alpha diversity among the three groups by Student's  t  test  (Fig.  7C-D).  A  Venn  diagram  shows  the intersections of the number of features shared by the three groups (Fig. 7E). An abundance histogram depicts species diversity and similarity in abundance at the phylum, class, order,  family,  genus,  and  species  levels  (Fig.  7F).  With respect to the phyla, classes, and orders, we found that RRx increased  the  MPTP-induced  reduction  in  the  abundance of the Deferribacterota phylum, Deferribacteres class, and

Deferribacterales  order.  In  addition,  a  phylogenetic  tree with a distribution of sample communities (Fig. 7H) and a ternary phase diagram (Fig. 7 I)	reveal	the	species	classifica -tion	of	the	five	phyla	with	the	greatest	abundance,	including the phylum Deferribacterota. The Wilcoxon rank-sum test indicated that the multigroup p value of the relative abundance of Deferribacterota was lower than 0.05 (Fig. 7G). Previous research has shown that the abundance of Deferribacterota is increased in NLRP3 knockout mice [38], indicating that this microbial phylum might be associated with NLRP3	inflammasome	activation.	These	results	suggested that	RRx	might	inhibit	NLRP3	inflammasome	activation	by increasing the abundance of Deferribacterota and attenuating fecal gut dysbiosis.

A  fecal  metabolomic  analysis  was  subsequently  performed	to	investigate	whether	RRx	affects	metabolism	in the gut of MPTP-induced PD mice. According to the PCA and OPLS-DA results, three groups could be clearly distinguished	 based	 on	 the	 metabolite	 expression	 profiles (Fig.  8 A-D).	 We	 further	 estimated	 these	 differences	 by generating  a  volcano  plot  based  on  the  variable  importance scores of the DEMs between the MPTP group and the  MPTP + RRx  group  (Fig.  8E).  The  top  20  DEMs based on the fold changes are displayed in the bar graph (Fig. 8F). The KEGG analysis of the MPTP group vs. the MPTP + RRx  group  revealed  the  enrichment  of  several representative	 pathways	 involving	 inflammation,	 oxida -tive stress, and neuroprotection (Fig. 8G). We generated an enrichment  chord  plot  to  visualize  the  DEMs  and  representative  pathways  obtained  from  the  KEGG  enrichment analysis (Fig. 8I), with the hierarchical clustering of these metabolites shown in Fig. 8H. Overall, the results of the 16 S rDNA sequencing analysis and metabolomic analysis of the fecal samples indicated that RRx altered the abundance and metabolism of the gut microbiota in the MPTP-induced PD model, providing mechanistic evidence for the neuroprotective	effect	of	RRx	on	the	gut-brain	axis.

We subsequently performed RNA sequencing to verify the	anti-inflammatory	effect	of	RRx	and	explore	its	underly -ing mechanism in the rescue of the degeneration, death, and loss of DAergic neurons. The mRNA level of Lcn2 measured by RNA sequencing tended to increase in the MPTP group compared with the control group, but this trend was reversed by RRx treatment. The Lcn2 gene encodes the neutrophil gelatinaseassociated protein LCN2 with multiple functions, and it has emerged	 as	 a	 pivotal	 regulator	 of	 neuroinflammation	 and oxidative stress in various CNS disorders [18, 40, 41]. Under pathological conditions such as cerebral ischaemia [20, 42, 43],  epilepsy  [44],  tau  pathology  concerning  neurodegeneration  [45],  cognitive  impairment  or  dementia  [21],  and PD [22, 24, 25 ],	LCN2	expression	is	significantly	increased in reactive astrocytes. Notably, recent studies have demonstrated that LCN2 can promote the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome-a	central	mediator	of	IL-1β	maturation	and pyroptosis-through	 NF-κB	 and	 24p3R	 receptor-depen -dent	signalling	pathways,	exacerbating	neuroinflammatory injury,  astrocyte  pyroptosis and neuronal dysfunction [20, 46,  47].  Moreover,  these  studies  showed  that  suppressing LCN2	 expression	 downregulated	 NLRP3	 inflammasome activation	and	astrocyte	pyroptosis.	Due	to	the	significant alteration  in  LCN2  protein  levels  in  our  analysis  and  the strong	 connection	 between	 LCN2-NLRP3	 inflammasome activation	 and	 neuroinflammation-mediated	 neurological disorders [18, 22, 23], we hypothesized that RRx targeted the	LCN2	protein	to	regulate	NLRP3	inflammasome	acti -vation	and	astrocyte	pyroptosis	and	consequently	influence PD progression. Our subsequent experimental results were consistent with our hypothesis. Further molecular docking analysis revealed that RRx targeted LCN2. The interaction between	LCN2	and	NLRP3	inflammasome	activation	con -tributes	to	neuroinflammation,	astrocyte	pyroptosis,	and	the loss of DAergic neurons, accelerating PD progression; these findings	highlight	the	effect	of	RRx	on	this	axis	as	a	promis -ing therapeutic target in PD pathogenesis.

Understanding  the  metabolic  changes  in  the  substantia  nigra of individuals with PD has emerged as a powerful  approach  for  elucidating  the  biochemical  mechanisms underpinning  PD  [48,  49].  Therefore,  we  conducted  a metabolomic  analysis  to  investigate  the  roles  of  metabolites  and  pathways  in  the  progression  of  PD.  The  results suggested that l-cysteine was closely linked to glutathione biosynthesis and metabolism, which decreased in PD mice but	 increased	 significantly	 in	 PD	 mice	 treated	 with	 RRx. L-cysteine plays a multifaceted role in neuroprotection by serving  as  a  precursor  for  glutathione  synthesis,  regulating redox homeostasis, and modulating metal ion chelation [50,  51]. The  induction  of  oxidative  stress  by  glutathione depletion is a central pathological feature in the substantia nigra of PD patients [5, 50, 51]. By promoting glutathione biosynthesis, L-cysteine may attenuate dopaminergic neurodegeneration by scavenging reactive oxygen species and preserving mitochondrial [36 ].	These	findings	suggest	that RRx  might  increase  the  level  of  the  metabolic  regulator L-cysteine	to	exert	antioxidant	effects	and	protect	DAergic neurons in individuals with PD. Moreover, two key DEMs, pyrophosphate and orthophosphate, were enriched in three significant	 metabolic	 pathways:	 PD,	 neurodegenerationmultiple diseases, and oxidative phosphorylation. Notably, these metabolites and pathways were clearly downregulated by  RRx treatment,  which  again  provided  strong  evidence that	RRx	has	an	antioxidant	effect	on	metabolism	in	the	sub -stantia nigra to reverse PD progression.

The gut-brain axis has attracted increasing attention in PD research,  with  compelling  evidence  linking  microbial dysbiosis	to	neuroinflammatory	responses	and	dopaminer -gic degeneration [37]. Metabolomics, when integrated with microbiota	profiling,	offers	a	systems-level	perspective	on host-microbe interactions, revealing functional metabolites that may mediate neurotoxicity or neuroprotection [49]. The 16 S rDNA analysis of fecal samples further complemented our  understanding  of  the  role  of  the  gut  microbiota  in modulating PD pathology. By employing high-throughput sequencing	techniques,	we	were	able	to	profile	gut	micro -bial	communities	and	identify	specific	taxa	that	were	differ -entially abundant in response to RRx treatment. Notably, the increase in the abundance of Deferribacterota, a microbial phylum	previously	linked	to	NLRP3	inflammasome	regula -tion, supports the hypothesis that RRx exerts its neuroprotective	effects	[ 38], at least in part, through modulation of the gut microbiome. Despite	these	significant	findings	and	meanings,	several limitations should be acknowledged. First, our understanding of the underlying mechanism of the gut-brain axis in the context of MPTP-induced neurodegeneration is limited. Although  changes  in  microbiota  abundance  and  metabolism	 were	 observed,	 the	 specific	 pathways	 facilitating	 the transition from gut to CNS pathology remain to be elucidated.	 Second,	 our	 focus	 was	 primarily	 on	 the	 effects	 of RRx on  the  CNS,  with  less  emphasis  on  how  peripheral immune responses connect with the brain. An examination of the potential role of RRx in shaping immune responses in peripheral organs is necessary. The absence of these data limits  our  understanding of broader systemic interactions. Third,  further  exploration  of  the  regulatory  mechanisms upstream of NLRP3 production is warranted. The broader role	of	the	anti-NLRP3	effect	of	RRx	on	systemic	immune responses	 and	 its	 involvement	 in	 both	 neuroinflammation and neurodegeneration should also be examined to aid in the development of therapeutic strategies. Finally, RRx may exert	 neuroprotective	 effects	 through	 other	 mechanisms, such as the direct regulation of neuronal degeneration and function	 or	 modulation	 of	 glia-neuron	 crosstalk.	 Future studies should explore these possibilities to fully elucidate the therapeutic potential of RRx as a treatment for PD.

## Conclusion

The	neuroprotective	effect	of	RRx	is	linked	to	the	suppres -sion	 of	 LCN2	 expression,	 which	 inhibits	 NLRP3	 inflam -masome activation-mediated astrocyte pyroptosis and neuroinflammation,	 consequently	 preventing	 the	 loss	 of DAergic neurons in a PD model. This study also highlights the	effects	of	RRx	on	altering	the	gut-brain	axis.	Although several	limitations	still	need	to	be	solved,	our	findings	sug -gest a promising therapeutic candidate for the treatment of PD and other neurodegenerative disorders.
